<p><b>India</b>, officially the <b>Republic of India</b> (<i>Bhārat Gaṇarājya</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[e]</a></sup> is a country in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_area" title="List of countries and dependencies by area">seventh-largest</a> country by area, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_dependencies_by_population" title="List of countries and dependencies by population">second-most populous</a> country (with over <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_India" title="Demographics of India">1.2 billion people</a>), and the most populous <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> in the world. It is bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> on the south, the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a> on the southwest, and the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> on the southeast. It shares land borders with <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> to the west;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[f]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> to the northeast; and <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar (Burma)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> to the east. In the Indian Ocean, India is in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>. India's <a href="/wiki/Andaman_and_Nicobar_Islands" title="Andaman and Nicobar Islands">Andaman and Nicobar Islands</a> share a maritime border with <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> was home to the urban <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilisation" title="Indus Valley Civilisation">Indus Valley Civilisation</a> of the 3rd millennium BCE — one of the world's <a href="/wiki/Cradle_of_civilization" title="Cradle of civilization">earliest civilizations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWright2010_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWright2010-25">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[g]</a></sup> In the following millennium, the oldest scriptures associated with <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> began to be composed. Large scale urbanization occurred on the Ganges in the first millennium BCE leading to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahajanapadas" title="Mahajanapadas">Mahajanapadas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a> arose. Early political consolidations took place under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maurya_empire" title="Maurya empire">Maurya</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gupta_empire" title="Gupta empire">Gupta empires</a>; the later peninsular <a href="/wiki/Middle_kingdoms_of_India#The_Deccan_plateau_and_South" title="Middle kingdoms of India">Middle Kingdoms</a> influenced cultures as far as <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. In the medieval era, <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrianism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> arrived, and <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a> emerged, all adding to the region's <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_India" title="Culture of India">diverse culture</a>. Much of the north fell to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delhi_sultanate" title="Delhi sultanate">Delhi sultanate</a>; the south was united under the <a href="/wiki/Vijayanagara_Empire" title="Vijayanagara Empire">Vijayanagara Empire</a>. The country was unified in the 17th century by the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal Empire</a>. In the 18th century, the subcontinent came under the <a href="/wiki/Maratha_Empire" title="Maratha Empire">Maratha Empire</a> and in the 19th under the British <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>, later shifting to British <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">crown rule</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_freedom_movement" title="Indian freedom movement">nationalist movement</a> emerged in the late 19th century, which later, under <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Mahatma Gandhi</a>, was noted for <a href="/wiki/Nonviolent_resistance" title="Nonviolent resistance">nonviolent resistance</a> and led to India's independence in 1947.</p>
<p>In 2017, the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_India" title="Economy of India">Indian economy</a> was the world's <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_past_and_projected_GDP_(nominal)" title="List of countries by past and projected GDP (nominal)">sixth largest</a> by nominal <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP)">third largest</a> by <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">purchasing power parity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imf2_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-imf2-20">[16]</a></sup> Following <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalisation_in_India" title="Economic liberalisation in India">market-based economic reforms</a> in 1991, India became one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_real_GDP_growth_rate" title="List of countries by real GDP growth rate">fastest-growing major economies</a> and is considered a <a href="/wiki/Newly_industrialized_country" title="Newly industrialized country">newly industrialised country</a>. However, it continues to face the challenges of <a href="/wiki/Poverty_in_India" title="Poverty in India">poverty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corruption_in_India" title="Corruption in India">corruption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition_in_India" title="Malnutrition in India">malnutrition</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_India" title="Healthcare in India">inadequate public healthcare</a>. A <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_with_nuclear_weapons" title="List of states with nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Power_(international_relations)#Power_as_status" title="Power (international relations)">regional power</a>, it has the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_number_of_military_and_paramilitary_personnel" title="List of countries by number of military and paramilitary personnel">second largest standing army</a> in the world and ranks <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_military_expenditures" title="List of countries by military expenditures">fifth in military expenditure</a> among nations. India is a <a href="/wiki/Federal_republic" title="Federal republic">federal republic</a> governed under a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary system</a> and consists of <a href="/wiki/States_and_union_territories_of_India" title="States and union territories of India">29 states and 7 union territories</a>. India is widely recognized for its wide <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_India" title="Cinema of India">cinema</a>, rich <a href="/wiki/Indian_cuisine" title="Indian cuisine">cuisine</a> and lush <a href="/wiki/Flora_of_India" title="Flora of India">wildlife and vegetation</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Pluralism_(political_philosophy)" title="Pluralism (political philosophy)">pluralistic</a>, multilingual and multi-ethnic society and is also home to a diversity of <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_of_India" title="Wildlife of India">wildlife</a> in a variety of <a href="/wiki/Protected_areas_of_India" title="Protected areas of India">protected habitats</a>.</p>

<p>The name <i>India</i> is derived from <i>Indus</i>, which originates from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Persian" title="Old Persian">Old Persian</a> word <i>Hindu</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESerge_Gruzinski2015_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESerge_Gruzinski2015-27">[20]</a></sup> The latter term stems from the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> word <i>Sindhu</i>, which was the historical local appellation for the <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">Indus River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOxford_English_Dictionary_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOxford_English_Dictionary-28">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greeks</a> referred to the Indians as <i>Indoi</i> (Ἰνδοί), which translates as "The people of the Indus".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKuiper201086_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKuiper201086-29">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The geographical term <i>Bharat</i> (<i lang="sa-Latn" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit transliteration">Bhārat</i>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Hindi_and_Urdu" title="Help:IPA/Hindi and Urdu">[ˈbʱaːrət̪]</a></span><small class="nowrap"> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Hi-Bharat.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Hi-Bharat.ogg" title="Hi-Bharat.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>), which is recognised by the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_India" title="Constitution of India">Constitution of India</a> as an official name for the country,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Law_and_Justice_2008_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Law_and_Justice_2008-30">[23]</a></sup> is used by <a href="/wiki/Names_of_India_in_its_official_languages" title="Names of India in its official languages">many Indian languages</a> in its variations. It is a modernisation of the historical name <i>Bharatavarsha</i>, which traditionally referred to the Indian subcontinent and gained increasing currency from the mid-19th century as a native name for India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clementin-Ojha_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clementin-Ojha-31">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrow_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barrow-32">[25]</a></sup> Scholars believe it to be named after the Vedic tribe of <i><a href="/wiki/Bh%C4%81ratas" title="Bhāratas">Bhāratas</a></i> in the second millennium BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scharfe_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scharfe-33">[26]</a></sup> It is also traditionally associated with the rule of the legendary emperor <a href="/wiki/Bharata_(emperor)" title="Bharata (emperor)">Bharata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[27]</a></sup> The Hindu text <a href="/wiki/Skanda_Purana" title="Skanda Purana">Skanda Purana</a> states that the region was named "Bharat" after <a href="/wiki/Bharata_Chakravartin" title="Bharata Chakravartin">Bharata Chakravartin</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Ga%E1%B9%87a_sangha" title="Gaṇa sangha">Gaṇarājya</a></i> (literally, people's State) is the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> term for "republic" dating back to ancient times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Hindustan" title="Hindustan">Hindustan</a></i> (<span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Hindi_and_Urdu" title="Help:IPA/Hindi and Urdu">[ɦɪnd̪ʊˈst̪aːn]</a></span><small class="nowrap"> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Hindustan.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/46/Hindustan.ogg" title="Hindustan.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> name for India dating back to the 3rd century BCE. It was introduced into India by the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughals</a> and widely used since then. Its meaning varied, referring to a region that encompassed northern India and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> or India in its entirety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clementin-Ojha_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Clementin-Ojha-31">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrow_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barrow-32">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEncyclopædia_Britannica_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEncyclopædia_Britannica-38">[31]</a></sup> Currently, the name may refer to either the northern part of India or the entire country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEncyclopædia_Britannica_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEncyclopædia_Britannica-38">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest authenticated <a href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_human" title="Anatomically modern human">human</a> remains in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> date to about 30,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPetragliaAllchin20076_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPetragliaAllchin20076-39">[32]</a></sup> Nearly contemporaneous <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> rock art sites have been found in many parts of the Indian subcontinent, including at the <a href="/wiki/Bhimbetka_rock_shelters" title="Bhimbetka rock shelters">Bhimbetka rock shelters</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh200989–93_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh200989–93-40">[33]</a></sup> Around 7000 BCE, one of the first known <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> settlements appeared on the subcontinent in <a href="/wiki/Mehrgarh" title="Mehrgarh">Mehrgarh</a> and other sites in the subcontinent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPossehl200324–25_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPossehl200324–25-41">[34]</a></sup> These gradually developed into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization" title="Indus Valley Civilization">Indus Valley Civilisation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200421–23_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200421–23-42">[35]</a></sup> the first urban culture in South Asia;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009181_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009181-43">[36]</a></sup> it flourished during 2500–1900 BCE in northeast Afghanistan to Pakistan and northwest India. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPossehl20032_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPossehl20032-44">[37]</a></sup> Centred around cities such as <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-daro" title="Mohenjo-daro">Mohenjo-daro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harappa" title="Harappa">Harappa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dholavira" title="Dholavira">Dholavira</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kalibangan" title="Kalibangan">Kalibangan</a>, and relying on varied forms of subsistence, the civilisation engaged robustly in crafts production and wide-ranging trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009181_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009181-43">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the period 2000–500 BCE, in terms of culture, many regions of the subcontinent transitioned from the <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009255_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009255-45">[38]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a>, the oldest scriptures associated with <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009186–187_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009186–187-46">[39]</a></sup> were composed during this period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWitzel200368–69_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWitzel200368–69-47">[40]</a></sup> and historians have analysed these to posit a <a href="/wiki/Vedic_period" title="Vedic period">Vedic culture</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punjab_region" title="Punjab region">Punjab region</a> and the upper <a href="/wiki/Indo-Gangetic_Plain" title="Indo-Gangetic Plain">Gangetic Plain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009255_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009255-45">[38]</a></sup> Most historians also consider this period to have encompassed several waves of <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_migration" title="Indo-Aryan migration">Indo-Aryan migration</a> into the subcontinent from the north-west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009186–187_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009186–187-46">[39]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caste_system" title="Caste system">caste system</a>, which created a hierarchy of priests, warriors, and free peasants, but which excluded indigenous peoples by labeling their occupations impure, arose during this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200441–43_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200441–43-48">[41]</a></sup> On the <a href="/wiki/Deccan_Plateau" title="Deccan Plateau">Deccan Plateau</a>, archaeological evidence from this period suggests the existence of a chiefdom stage of political organisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009255_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009255-45">[38]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a>, a progression to sedentary life is indicated by the large number of <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megalithic</a> monuments dating from this period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009250–251_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009250–251-49">[42]</a></sup> as well as by nearby traces of agriculture, irrigation tanks, and craft traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009250–251_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009250–251-49">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late <a href="/wiki/Vedic_period" title="Vedic period">Vedic period</a>, around the 6th century BCE, the small states and chiefdoms of the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a> Plain and the north-western regions had consolidated into 16 major oligarchies and monarchies that were known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahajanapada" title="Mahajanapada">mahajanapadas</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009260–265_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009260–265-50">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200453–54_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200453–54-51">[44]</a></sup> The emerging urbanisation gave rise to non-Vedic religious movements, two of which became independent religions. Jainism came into prominence during the life of its exemplar, <a href="/wiki/Mahavira" title="Mahavira">Mahavira</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009312–313_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009312–313-52">[45]</a></sup> Buddhism, based on the teachings of <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Gautama Buddha</a>, attracted followers from all social classes excepting the middle class; chronicling the life of the Buddha was central to the beginnings of recorded history in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200454–56_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200454–56-53">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein199821_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein199821-54">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein199867–68_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein199867–68-55">[48]</a></sup> In an age of increasing urban wealth, both religions held up <a href="/wiki/Nekkhamma" title="Nekkhamma">renunciation</a> as an ideal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009300_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009300-56">[49]</a></sup> and both established long-lasting monastic traditions. Politically, by the 3rd century BCE, the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Magadha" title="Magadha">Magadha</a> had annexed or reduced other states to emerge as the <a href="/wiki/Maurya_Empire" title="Maurya Empire">Mauryan Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009319_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009319-57">[50]</a></sup> The empire was once thought to have controlled most of the subcontinent excepting the far south, but its core regions are now thought to have been separated by large autonomous areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein199878–79_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein199878–79-58">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200470_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200470-59">[52]</a></sup> The Mauryan kings are known as much for their empire-building and determined management of public life as for <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a>'s renunciation of militarism and far-flung advocacy of the Buddhist <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dharma_(Buddhism)" title="Dharma (Buddhism)">dhamma</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009367_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009367-60">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200463_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200463-61">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sangam_literature" title="Sangam literature">Sangam literature</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil language</a> reveals that, between 200 BCE and 200 CE, the southern peninsula was being ruled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chera_Dynasty" title="Chera Dynasty">Cheras</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chola_dynasty" title="Chola dynasty">Cholas</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pandyan_Dynasty" title="Pandyan Dynasty">Pandyas</a>, dynasties that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Roman_trade" title="Indo-Roman trade">traded extensively with the Roman Empire</a> and with <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">South-East Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein199889–90_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein199889–90-62">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009408–415_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009408–415-63">[56]</a></sup> In North India, Hinduism asserted patriarchal control within the family, leading to increased subordination of women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein199892–95_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein199892–95-64">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009319_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009319-57">[50]</a></sup> By the 4th and 5th centuries, the <a href="/wiki/Gupta_Empire" title="Gupta Empire">Gupta Empire</a> had created in the greater Ganges Plain a complex system of administration and taxation that became a model for later Indian kingdoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200489–91_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund200489–91-65">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009545_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009545-66">[59]</a></sup> Under the Guptas, a renewed Hinduism based on devotion rather than the management of ritual began to assert itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein199898–99_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein199898–99-67">[60]</a></sup> The renewal was reflected in a flowering of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sculpture_in_the_Indian_subcontinent" title="Sculpture in the Indian subcontinent">sculpture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Architecture_of_India" title="Architecture of India">architecture</a>, which found patrons among an urban elite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009545_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009545-66">[59]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_literature#Classical_Sanskrit_literature" title="Sanskrit literature">Classical Sanskrit literature</a> flowered as well, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_and_technology_in_the_Indian_subcontinent" title="History of science and technology in the Indian subcontinent">Indian science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_astronomy" title="Indian astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">medicine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indian_mathematics" title="Indian mathematics">mathematics</a> made significant advances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESingh2009545_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2009545-66">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Indian early medieval age, 600 CE to 1200 CE, is defined by regional kingdoms and cultural diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998132_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998132-68">[61]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Harsha" title="Harsha">Harsha</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kannauj" title="Kannauj">Kannauj</a>, who ruled much of the Indo-Gangetic Plain from 606 to 647 CE, attempted to expand southwards, he was defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Chalukya_dynasty" title="Chalukya dynasty">Chalukya</a> ruler of the Deccan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998119–120_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998119–120-69">[62]</a></sup> When his successor attempted to expand eastwards, he was defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Pala_Empire" title="Pala Empire">Pala</a> king of <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998119–120_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998119–120-69">[62]</a></sup> When the Chalukyas attempted to expand southwards, they were defeated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pallava" title="Pallava">Pallavas</a> from farther south, who in turn were opposed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pandyas" title="Pandyas">Pandyas</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cholas" title="Cholas">Cholas</a> from still farther south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998119–120_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998119–120-69">[62]</a></sup> No ruler of this period was able to create an empire and consistently control lands much beyond his core region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998132_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998132-68">[61]</a></sup> During this time, pastoral peoples whose land had been cleared to make way for the growing agricultural economy were accommodated within caste society, as were new non-traditional ruling classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998121–122_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998121–122-70">[63]</a></sup> The caste system consequently began to show regional differences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998121–122_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998121–122-70">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 6th and 7th centuries, the first <a href="/wiki/Bhakti" title="Bhakti">devotional hymns</a> were created in the Tamil language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998123_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998123-71">[64]</a></sup> They were imitated all over India and led to both the resurgence of Hinduism and the development of all <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">modern languages of the subcontinent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998123_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998123-71">[64]</a></sup> Indian royalty, big and small, and the temples they patronised, drew citizens in great numbers to the capital cities, which became economic hubs as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998124_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998124-72">[65]</a></sup> Temple towns of various sizes began to appear everywhere as India underwent another urbanisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998124_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998124-72">[65]</a></sup> By the 8th and 9th centuries, the effects were felt in South-East Asia, as South Indian culture and political systems were exported to lands that became part of modern-day <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998127–128_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998127–128-73">[66]</a></sup> Indian merchants, scholars, and sometimes armies were involved in this transmission; South-East Asians took the initiative as well, with many sojourning in Indian seminaries and translating Buddhist and Hindu texts into their languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998127–128_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998127–128-73">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the 10th century, Muslim Central Asian nomadic clans, using <a href="/wiki/Courser_(horse)" title="Courser (horse)">swift-horse</a> cavalry and raising vast armies united by ethnicity and religion, repeatedly overran South Asia's north-western plains, leading eventually to the establishment of the Islamic <a href="/wiki/Delhi_Sultanate" title="Delhi Sultanate">Delhi Sultanate</a> in 1206.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELudden200268_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELudden200268-74">[67]</a></sup> The sultanate was to control much of North India and to make many forays into South India. Although at first disruptive for the Indian elites, the sultanate largely left its vast non-Muslim subject population to its own laws and customs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot200847_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot200847-75">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf20066_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf20066-76">[69]</a></sup> By repeatedly repulsing <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongol raiders</a> in the 13th century, the sultanate saved India from the devastation visited on West and Central Asia, setting the scene for centuries of <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">migration</a> of fleeing soldiers, learned men, mystics, traders, artists, and artisans from that region into the subcontinent, thereby creating a syncretic Indo-Islamic culture in the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELudden200267_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELudden200267-77">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot200850–51_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot200850–51-78">[71]</a></sup> The sultanate's raiding and weakening of the regional kingdoms of South India paved the way for the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Vijayanagara_Empire" title="Vijayanagara Empire">Vijayanagara Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot200853_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot200853-79">[72]</a></sup> Embracing a strong <a href="/wiki/Shaivism" title="Shaivism">Shaivite</a> tradition and building upon the military technology of the sultanate, the empire came to control much of peninsular India,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200612_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200612-80">[73]</a></sup> and was to influence South Indian society for long afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot200853_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot200853-79">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 16th century, northern India, being then under mainly Muslim rulers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobb200180_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobb200180-81">[74]</a></sup> fell again to the superior mobility and firepower of a new generation of Central Asian warriors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998164_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998164-82">[75]</a></sup> The resulting <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal Empire</a> did not stamp out the local societies it came to rule, but rather balanced and pacified them through new administrative practices<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008115_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008115-83">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobb200190–91_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobb200190–91-84">[77]</a></sup> and diverse and inclusive ruling elites,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200617_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200617-85">[78]</a></sup> leading to more systematic, centralised, and uniform rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008152_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008152-86">[79]</a></sup> Eschewing tribal bonds and Islamic identity, especially under <a href="/wiki/Akbar" title="Akbar">Akbar</a>, the Mughals united their far-flung realms through loyalty, expressed through a Persianised culture, to an emperor who had near-divine status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200617_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200617-85">[78]</a></sup> The Mughal state's economic policies, deriving most revenues from agriculture<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008158_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008158-87">[80]</a></sup> and mandating that taxes be paid in the well-regulated silver currency,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998169_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998169-88">[81]</a></sup> caused peasants and artisans to enter larger markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008152_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008152-86">[79]</a></sup> The relative peace maintained by the empire during much of the 17th century was a factor in India's economic expansion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008152_86-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008152-86">[79]</a></sup> resulting in greater patronage of <a href="/wiki/Mughal_painting" title="Mughal painting">painting</a>, literary forms, textiles, and <a href="/wiki/Mughal_architecture" title="Mughal architecture">architecture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008186_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008186-89">[82]</a></sup> Newly coherent social groups in northern and western India, such as the <a href="/wiki/Maratha_Empire" title="Maratha Empire">Marathas</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rajputs" title="Rajputs">Rajputs</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sikh_empire" title="Sikh empire">Sikhs</a>, gained military and governing ambitions during Mughal rule, which, through collaboration or adversity, gave them both recognition and military experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200623–24_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200623–24-90">[83]</a></sup> Expanding commerce during Mughal rule gave rise to new Indian commercial and political elites along the coasts of southern and eastern India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200623–24_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200623–24-90">[83]</a></sup> As the empire disintegrated, many among these elites were able to seek and control their own affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008256_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008256-91">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the early 18th century, with the lines between commercial and political dominance being increasingly blurred, a number of European trading companies, including the English <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>, had established coastal outposts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008286_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008286-92">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200644–49_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200644–49-93">[86]</a></sup> The East India Company's control of the seas, greater resources, and more advanced military training and technology led it to increasingly flex its military muscle and caused it to become attractive to a portion of the Indian elite; these factors were crucial in allowing the company to gain control over the <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a> region by 1765 and sideline the other European companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobb200198–100_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobb200198–100-94">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008286_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008286-92">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELudden2002128–132_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELudden2002128–132-95">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200651–55_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200651–55-96">[89]</a></sup> Its further access to the riches of Bengal and the subsequent increased strength and size of its army enabled it to annex or subdue most of India by the 1820s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200668–71_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200668–71-97">[90]</a></sup> India was then no longer exporting manufactured goods as it long had, but was instead supplying the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> with raw materials, and many historians consider this to be the onset of India's colonial period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008286_92-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008286-92">[85]</a></sup> By this time, with its economic power severely curtailed by the British parliament and effectively having been made an arm of British administration, the company began to more consciously enter non-economic arenas such as education, social reform, and culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008289_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsherTalbot2008289-98">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historians consider India's modern age to have begun sometime between 1848 and 1885. The appointment in 1848 of <a href="/wiki/James_Broun-Ramsay,_1st_Marquess_of_Dalhousie" title="James Broun-Ramsay, 1st Marquess of Dalhousie">Lord Dalhousie</a> as Governor General of the East India Company set the stage for changes essential to a modern state. These included the consolidation and demarcation of sovereignty, the surveillance of the population, and the education of citizens. Technological changes—among them, railways, canals, and the telegraph—were introduced not long after their introduction in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobb2001151–152_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobb2001151–152-99">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200694–99_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200694–99-100">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrown199483_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown199483-101">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeers200650_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeers200650-102">[95]</a></sup> However, disaffection with the company also grew during this time, and set off the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Rebellion_of_1857" title="Indian Rebellion of 1857">Indian Rebellion of 1857</a>. Fed by diverse resentments and perceptions, including invasive British-style social reforms, harsh land taxes, and summary treatment of some rich landowners and princes, the rebellion rocked many regions of northern and central India and shook the foundations of Company rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006100–103_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006100–103-103">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrown199485–86_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown199485–86-104">[97]</a></sup> Although the rebellion was suppressed by 1858, it led to the dissolution of the East India Company and to the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">direct administration of India</a> by the British government. Proclaiming a unitary state and a gradual but limited British-style parliamentary system, the new rulers also protected princes and landed gentry as a feudal safeguard against future unrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998239_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998239-105">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006103–108_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006103–108-106">[99]</a></sup> In the decades following, public life gradually emerged all over India, leading eventually to the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Congress" title="Indian National Congress">Indian National Congress</a> in 1885.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobb2001183_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobb2001183-107">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESarkar19831–4_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESarkar19831–4-108">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECopland2001ix–x_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopland2001ix–x-109">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006123_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006123-110">[103]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rush of technology and the commercialisation of agriculture in the second half of the 19th century was marked by economic setbacks—many small farmers became dependent on the whims of far-away markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998260_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998260-111">[104]</a></sup> There was an increase in the number of large-scale <a href="/wiki/Famine_in_India" title="Famine in India">famines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoseJalal2011117_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoseJalal2011117-112">[105]</a></sup> and, despite the risks of infrastructure development borne by Indian taxpayers, little industrial employment was generated for Indians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStein1998258_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStein1998258-113">[106]</a></sup> There were also salutary effects: commercial cropping, especially in the newly canalled Punjab, led to increased food production for internal consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006126_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006126-114">[107]</a></sup> The railway network provided critical famine relief,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200697_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200697-115">[108]</a></sup> notably reduced the cost of moving goods,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200697_115-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf200697-115">[108]</a></sup> and helped nascent Indian-owned industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006126_114-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006126-114">[107]</a></sup></p>
<p>After World War I, in which approximately <a href="/wiki/Indian_Army_during_World_War_I" title="Indian Army during World War I">one million Indians served</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006163_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006163-116">[109]</a></sup> a new period began. It was marked by <a href="/wiki/Montagu%E2%80%93Chelmsford_Reforms" title="Montagu–Chelmsford Reforms">British reforms</a> but also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rowlatt_act" title="Rowlatt act">repressive legislations</a>, by more strident Indian calls for self-rule, and by the beginnings of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonviolent" title="Nonviolent">nonviolent</a> movement of non-co-operation, of which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohandas_Karamchand_Gandhi" title="Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi">Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi</a> would become the leader and enduring symbol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006167_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006167-117">[110]</a></sup> During the 1930s, slow legislative reform was enacted by the British; the Indian National Congress won victories in the resulting elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006195–197_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006195–197-118">[111]</a></sup> The next decade was beset with crises: <a href="/wiki/India_in_World_War_II" title="India in World War II">Indian participation in World War II</a>, the Congress's final push for non-co-operation, and an upsurge of Muslim nationalism. All were capped by the advent of independence in 1947, but tempered by the <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_India" title="Partition of India">partition of India</a> into two states: India and Pakistan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006203_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006203-119">[112]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vital to India's self-image as an independent nation was its constitution, completed in 1950, which put in place a secular and democratic republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006231_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006231-120">[113]</a></sup> It has remained a democracy with civil liberties, an active Supreme Court, and a largely independent press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006265–266_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006265–266-121">[114]</a></sup> Economic liberalisation, which was begun in the 1990s, has created a large urban middle class, transformed India into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(real)_growth_rate" title="List of countries by GDP (real) growth rate">one of the world's fastest-growing economies</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnited_States_Department_of_Agriculture_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnited_States_Department_of_Agriculture-122">[115]</a></sup> and increased its geopolitical clout. Indian movies, music, and spiritual teachings play an increasing role in global culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006265–266_121-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006265–266-121">[114]</a></sup> Yet, India is also shaped by seemingly unyielding poverty, both rural and urban;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006265–266_121-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006265–266-121">[114]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Religious_violence_in_India" title="Religious violence in India">religious</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caste-related_violence_in_India" title="Caste-related violence in India">caste-related violence</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006266–270_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006266–270-123">[116]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Naxalite" title="Naxalite">Maoist-inspired Naxalite insurgencies</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006253_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006253-124">[117]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Insurgency_in_Jammu_and_Kashmir" title="Insurgency in Jammu and Kashmir">separatism in Jammu and Kashmir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insurgency_in_Northeast_India" title="Insurgency in Northeast India">in Northeast India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006274_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006274-125">[118]</a></sup> It has unresolved territorial disputes with <a href="/wiki/China%E2%80%93India_relations#1960s" title="China–India relations">China</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006247–248_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006247–248-126">[119]</a></sup> and with <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_wars_and_conflicts" title="Indo-Pakistani wars and conflicts">Pakistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006247–248_126-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006247–248-126">[119]</a></sup> The India–Pakistan nuclear rivalry came to a head in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006293–295_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006293–295-127">[120]</a></sup> India's sustained democratic freedoms are unique among the world's newer nations; however, in spite of its recent economic successes, freedom from want for its disadvantaged population remains a goal yet to be achieved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006304_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMetcalfMetcalf2006304-128">[121]</a></sup></p>
<p>India comprises the bulk of the Indian subcontinent, lying atop the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Plate" title="Indian Plate">Indian tectonic plate</a>, and part of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Australian_Plate" title="Indo-Australian Plate">Indo-Australian Plate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliAitchison2005_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliAitchison2005-129">[122]</a></sup> India's defining geological processes began 75 million years ago when the Indian plate, then part of the southern supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>, began a north-eastward <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">drift</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a> to its south-west, and later, south and south-east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliAitchison2005_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliAitchison2005-129">[122]</a></sup> Simultaneously, the vast <a href="/wiki/Tethys_Ocean" title="Tethys Ocean">Tethyn</a> <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a>, to its northeast, began to <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduct</a> under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurasian_plate" title="Eurasian plate">Eurasian plate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliAitchison2005_129-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliAitchison2005-129">[122]</a></sup> These dual processes, driven by convection in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, both created the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> and caused the Indian <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a> eventually to under-thrust Eurasia and to uplift the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAliAitchison2005_129-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAliAitchison2005-129">[122]</a></sup> Immediately south of the emerging Himalayas, plate movement created a vast <a href="/wiki/Trough_(geology)" title="Trough (geology)">trough</a> that rapidly filled with river-borne sediment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg7_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg7-130">[123]</a></sup> and now constitutes the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Gangetic_Plain" title="Indo-Gangetic Plain">Indo-Gangetic Plain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPrakash_et_al.2000_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPrakash_et_al.2000-131">[124]</a></sup> Cut off from the plain by the ancient <a href="/wiki/Aravalli_Range" title="Aravalli Range">Aravalli Range</a> lies the <a href="/wiki/Thar_Desert" title="Thar Desert">Thar Desert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg11_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg11-132">[125]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original Indian plate survives as <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">peninsular India</a>, the oldest and geologically most stable part of India. It extends as far north as the <a href="/wiki/Satpura_Range" title="Satpura Range">Satpura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vindhya_Range" title="Vindhya Range">Vindhya</a> ranges in central India. These parallel chains run from the Arabian Sea coast in Gujarat in the west to the coal-rich <a href="/wiki/Chota_Nagpur_Plateau" title="Chota Nagpur Plateau">Chota Nagpur Plateau</a> in Jharkhand in the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg8_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg8-133">[126]</a></sup> To the south, the remaining peninsular landmass, the <a href="/wiki/Deccan_Plateau" title="Deccan Plateau">Deccan Plateau</a>, is flanked on the west and east by coastal ranges known as the <a href="/wiki/Western_Ghats" title="Western Ghats">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Ghats" title="Eastern Ghats">Eastern Ghats</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg9–10_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg9–10-134">[127]</a></sup> the plateau contains the country's oldest rock formations, some over one billion years old. Constituted in such fashion, India lies to the north of the equator between 6° 44' and 35° 30' north latitude<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[h]</a></sup> and 68° 7' and 97° 25' east longitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Information_and_Broadcasting20071_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Information_and_Broadcasting20071-136">[128]</a></sup></p>
<p>India's coastline measures 7,517 kilometres (4,700 mi) in length; of this distance, 5,423 kilometres (3,400 mi) belong to peninsular India and 2,094 kilometres (1,300 mi) to the Andaman, Nicobar, and Lakshadweep island chains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKumarPathakPednekarRaju2006_137-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKumarPathakPednekarRaju2006-137">[129]</a></sup> According to the Indian naval hydrographic charts, the mainland coastline consists of the following: 43% sandy beaches; 11% rocky shores, including cliffs; and 46% <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">mudflats</a> or marshy shores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKumarPathakPednekarRaju2006_137-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKumarPathakPednekarRaju2006-137">[129]</a></sup></p>
<p>Major Himalayan-origin rivers that substantially flow through India include the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Brahmaputra_River" title="Brahmaputra River">Brahmaputra</a>, both of which drain into the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg15_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg15-138">[130]</a></sup> Important tributaries of the Ganges include the <a href="/wiki/Yamuna" title="Yamuna">Yamuna</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosi_River" title="Kosi River">Kosi</a>; the latter's extremely low gradient often leads to severe floods and course changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDuff1993353_139-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDuff1993353-139">[131]</a></sup> Major peninsular rivers, whose steeper gradients prevent their waters from flooding, include the <a href="/wiki/Godavari_River" title="Godavari River">Godavari</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahanadi_River" title="Mahanadi River">Mahanadi</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaveri_River" title="Kaveri River">Kaveri</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Krishna_River" title="Krishna River">Krishna</a>, which also drain into the Bay of Bengal;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg16_140-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg16-140">[132]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Narmada_River" title="Narmada River">Narmada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tapti_River" title="Tapti River">Tapti</a>, which drain into the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg17_141-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg17-141">[133]</a></sup> Coastal features include the marshy <a href="/wiki/Rann_of_Kutch" title="Rann of Kutch">Rann of Kutch</a> of western India and the alluvial <a href="/wiki/Sundarbans" title="Sundarbans">Sundarbans</a> delta of eastern India; the latter is shared with Bangladesh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg12_142-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg12-142">[134]</a></sup> India has two <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelagos</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Lakshadweep" title="Lakshadweep">Lakshadweep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">coral atolls</a> off India's south-western coast; and the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, a volcanic chain in the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Sea" title="Andaman Sea">Andaman Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg13_143-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDikshit_&amp;_Schwartzberg13-143">[135]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_India" title="Climate of India">Indian climate</a> is strongly influenced by the Himalayas and the Thar Desert, both of which drive the economically and culturally pivotal summer and winter <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChang1967391–394_144-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChang1967391–394-144">[136]</a></sup> The Himalayas prevent cold Central Asian <a href="/wiki/Katabatic_wind" title="Katabatic wind">katabatic winds</a> from blowing in, keeping the bulk of the Indian subcontinent warmer than most locations at similar latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPosey1994118_145-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPosey1994118-145">[137]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolpert20034_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolpert20034-146">[138]</a></sup> The Thar Desert plays a crucial role in attracting the moisture-laden south-west summer monsoon winds that, between June and October, provide the majority of India's rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChang1967391–394_144-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChang1967391–394-144">[136]</a></sup> Four major climatic groupings predominate in India: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_India#Tropical_wet" title="Climate of India">tropical wet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_India#Tropical_dry" title="Climate of India">tropical dry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_India#Subtropical_humid" title="Climate of India">subtropical humid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_India#Mountain" title="Climate of India">montane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeitzmanWorden199697_147-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeitzmanWorden199697-147">[139]</a></sup></p>
<p>India lies within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indomalaya_ecozone" title="Indomalaya ecozone">Indomalaya ecozone</a> and contains three <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_hotspot" title="Biodiversity hotspot">biodiversity hotspots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConservation_International2007_149-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConservation_International2007-149">[141]</a></sup> One of 17 <a href="/wiki/Megadiverse_countries" title="Megadiverse countries">megadiverse countries</a>, it hosts 8.6% of all mammalian, 13.7% of all avian, 7.9% of all reptilian, 6% of all amphibian, 12.2% of all piscine, and 6.0% of all flowering plant species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZoological_Survey_of_India20121_150-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZoological_Survey_of_India20121-150">[142]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPuri_151-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPuri-151">[143]</a></sup> About 21.2% of the country's landmass is covered by forests (tree canopy density &gt;10%), of which 12.2% comprises moderately or very dense forests (tree canopy density &gt;40%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForest_Survey_of_India201311–14_152-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForest_Survey_of_India201311–14-152">[144]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">Endemism</a> is high among plants, 33%, and among <a href="/wiki/List_of_ecoregions_in_India" title="List of ecoregions in India">ecoregions</a> such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Shola" title="Shola">shola</a></i> <a href="/wiki/South_Western_Ghats_montane_rain_forests" title="South Western Ghats montane rain forests">forests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBasak198324_153-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBasak198324-153">[145]</a></sup> Habitat ranges from the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_moist_broadleaf_forests" title="Tropical and subtropical moist broadleaf forests">tropical rainforest</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">Andaman Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Ghats" title="Western Ghats">Western Ghats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northeast_India" title="Northeast India">North-East India</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Temperate_coniferous_forest" title="Temperate coniferous forest">coniferous forest</a> of the Himalaya. Between these extremes lie the moist deciduous <i><a href="/wiki/Shorea_robusta" title="Shorea robusta">sal</a></i> forest of eastern India; the dry deciduous <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a> forest of central and southern India; and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acacia_nilotica" title="Acacia nilotica">babul</a>-dominated <a href="/wiki/Deserts_and_xeric_shrublands" title="Deserts and xeric shrublands">thorn forest</a> of the central Deccan and western Gangetic plain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETritsch2001_154-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETritsch2001-154">[146]</a></sup> The medicinal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neem" title="Neem">neem</a>, widely used in rural Indian herbal remedies, is a key Indian tree. The luxuriant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_fig" title="Sacred fig">pipal</a> fig tree, shown on the seals of <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-daro" title="Mohenjo-daro">Mohenjo-daro</a>, shaded Gautama Buddha as he sought enlightenment.</p>
<p>Many Indian species descend from <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> originating in Gondwana, from which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_plate" title="Indian plate">Indian plate</a> separated more than 105 million years <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">before present</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrameOwen2002142_155-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrameOwen2002142-155">[147]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Indian_Plate" title="Indian Plate">Peninsular India</a>'s subsequent <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">movement</a> towards and collision with the <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasian</a> landmass set off a mass exchange of species. <a href="/wiki/Deccan_Traps" title="Deccan Traps">Epochal volcanism</a> and climatic changes 20 million years ago forced a mass extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKaranth2006_156-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKaranth2006-156">[148]</a></sup> Mammals then entered India from Asia through two <a href="/wiki/Zoogeography" title="Zoogeography">zoogeographical</a> passes flanking the rising Himalaya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETritsch2001_154-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETritsch2001-154">[146]</a></sup> Thus, while 45.8% of reptiles and 55.8% of amphibians are endemic, only 12.6% of mammals and 4.5% of birds are.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPuri_151-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPuri-151">[143]</a></sup> Among them are the <a href="/wiki/Nilgiri_langur" title="Nilgiri langur">Nilgiri leaf monkey</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bufo_beddomii" title="Bufo beddomii">Beddome's toad</a> of the Western Ghats. India contains 172 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Conservation_Union" title="World Conservation Union">IUCN</a>-designated <a href="/wiki/List_of_endangered_animals_in_India" title="List of endangered animals in India">threatened animal species</a>, or 2.9% of endangered forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMace19944_157-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMace19944-157">[149]</a></sup> These include the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic_lion" title="Asiatic lion">Asiatic lion</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bengal_tiger" title="Bengal tiger">Bengal tiger</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Snow_leopard" title="Snow leopard">snow leopard</a> and the <a href="/wiki/White-rumped_vulture" title="White-rumped vulture">Indian white-rumped vulture</a>, which, by ingesting the carrion of <a href="/wiki/Diclofenac" title="Diclofenac">diclofenac</a>-laced cattle, nearly became extinct.</p>
<p>The pervasive and ecologically devastating human encroachment of recent decades has critically endangered Indian wildlife. In response, the system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_parks_of_India" title="National parks of India">national parks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protected_areas_of_India" title="Protected areas of India">protected areas</a>, first established in 1935, was substantially expanded. In 1972, India enacted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildlife_Protection_Act_of_1972" title="Wildlife Protection Act of 1972">Wildlife Protection Act</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Environments_and_Forests_1972_158-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Environments_and_Forests_1972-158">[150]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Project_Tiger" title="Project Tiger">Project Tiger</a> to safeguard crucial wilderness; the Forest Conservation Act was enacted in 1980 and amendments added in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDepartment_of_Environment_and_Forests1988_159-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDepartment_of_Environment_and_Forests1988-159">[151]</a></sup> India hosts <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_sanctuaries_of_India" title="Wildlife sanctuaries of India">more than five hundred wildlife sanctuaries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biosphere_reserves_of_India" title="Biosphere reserves of India">thirteen biosphere reserves</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Environment_and_Forests_160-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Environment_and_Forests-160">[152]</a></sup> four of which are part of the <a href="/wiki/World_Network_of_Biosphere_Reserves" title="World Network of Biosphere Reserves">World Network of Biosphere Reserves</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Ramsar_Sites_in_India" title="List of Ramsar Sites in India">twenty-five wetlands</a> are registered under the <a href="/wiki/Ramsar_Convention" title="Ramsar Convention">Ramsar Convention</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESecretariat_of_the_Convention_on_Wetlands_161-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESecretariat_of_the_Convention_on_Wetlands-161">[153]</a></sup></p>
<p>India is the world's most populous democracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnited_Nations_Population_Division_162-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnited_Nations_Population_Division-162">[154]</a></sup> A parliamentary republic with a <a href="/wiki/Multi-party_system" title="Multi-party system">multi-party system</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurnellCalvert1999125_163-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurnellCalvert1999125-163">[155]</a></sup> it has seven <a href="/wiki/Election_Commission_of_India" title="Election Commission of India">recognised</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_recognised_political_parties_in_India#National" title="List of recognised political parties in India">national parties</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Congress" title="Indian National Congress">Indian National Congress</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bharatiya_Janata_Party" title="Bharatiya Janata Party">Bharatiya Janata Party</a> (BJP), and more than 40 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_recognised_political_parties_in_India#State" title="List of recognised political parties in India">regional parties</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEElection_Commission_of_India_164-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEElection_Commission_of_India-164">[156]</a></sup> The Congress is considered centre-left in Indian <a href="/wiki/Political_culture" title="Political culture">political culture</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[157]</a></sup> and the BJP <a href="/wiki/Right-wing_politics" title="Right-wing politics">right-wing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalikSingh1992318–336_166-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalikSingh1992318–336-166">[158]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC2012_167-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC2012-167">[159]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBanerjee20053118_168-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBanerjee20053118-168">[160]</a></sup> For most of the period between 1950—when India first became a republic—and the late 1980s, the Congress held a majority in the parliament. Since then, however, it has increasingly shared the political stage with the BJP,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESarkar200784_169-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESarkar200784-169">[161]</a></sup> as well as with powerful regional parties which have often forced the creation of multi-party <a href="/wiki/Coalition_government" title="Coalition government">coalitions</a> at the centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChander2004117_170-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChander2004117-170">[162]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Republic of India's first three general elections, in 1951, 1957, and 1962, the <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru" title="Jawaharlal Nehru">Jawaharlal Nehru</a>-led Congress won easy victories. On Nehru's death in 1964, <a href="/wiki/Lal_Bahadur_Shastri" title="Lal Bahadur Shastri">Lal Bahadur Shastri</a> briefly became prime minister; he was succeeded, after his own unexpected death in 1966, by <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi" title="Indira Gandhi">Indira Gandhi</a>, who went on to lead the Congress to election victories in 1967 and 1971. Following public discontent with the <a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(India)" title="The Emergency (India)">state of emergency</a> she declared in 1975, the Congress was voted out of power in 1977; the then-new <a href="/wiki/Janata_Party" title="Janata Party">Janata Party</a>, which had opposed the emergency, was voted in. Its government lasted just over three years. Voted back into power in 1980, the Congress saw a change in leadership in 1984, when Indira Gandhi was assassinated; she was succeeded by her son <a href="/wiki/Rajiv_Gandhi" title="Rajiv Gandhi">Rajiv Gandhi</a>, who won an easy victory in the general elections later that year. The Congress was voted out again in 1989 when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Front_(India)" title="National Front (India)">National Front</a> coalition, led by the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Janata_Dal" title="Janata Dal">Janata Dal</a> in alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_India_(Marxist)" title="Communist Party of India (Marxist)">Left Front</a>, won the elections; that government too proved relatively short-lived, lasting just under two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBhambhri1992118,_143_171-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBhambhri1992118,_143-171">[163]</a></sup> Elections were held again in 1991; no party won an absolute majority. The Congress, as the largest single party, was able to form a <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority government</a> led by <a href="/wiki/P._V._Narasimha_Rao" title="P. V. Narasimha Rao">P. V. Narasimha Rao</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Hindu_2008_172-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Hindu_2008-172">[164]</a></sup></p>
<p>A two-year period of political turmoil followed the general election of 1996. Several short-lived alliances shared power at the centre. The BJP formed a government briefly in 1996; it was followed by two comparatively long-lasting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Front_(India)" title="United Front (India)">United Front</a> coalitions, which depended on external support. In 1998, the BJP was able to form a successful coalition, the <a href="/wiki/National_Democratic_Alliance_(India)" title="National Democratic Alliance (India)">National Democratic Alliance</a> (NDA). Led by <a href="/wiki/Atal_Bihari_Vajpayee" title="Atal Bihari Vajpayee">Atal Bihari Vajpayee</a>, the NDA became the first non-Congress, <a href="/wiki/Coalition_government" title="Coalition government">coalition government</a> to complete a five-year term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDunleavyDiwakarDunleavy2007_173-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDunleavyDiwakarDunleavy2007-173">[165]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Indian_general_election,_2004" title="Indian general election, 2004">2004 Indian general elections</a>, again no party won an absolute majority, but the Congress emerged as the largest single party, forming another successful coalition: the <a href="/wiki/United_Progressive_Alliance" title="United Progressive Alliance">United Progressive Alliance</a> (UPA). It had the support of left-leaning parties and MPs who opposed the BJP. The UPA returned to power in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_general_election,_2009" title="Indian general election, 2009">2009 general election</a> with increased numbers, and it no longer required external support from <a href="/wiki/Socialism_in_India" title="Socialism in India">India's communist parties</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund2004384_174-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKulkeRothermund2004384-174">[166]</a></sup> That year, <a href="/wiki/Manmohan_Singh" title="Manmohan Singh">Manmohan Singh</a> became the first prime minister since <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru" title="Jawaharlal Nehru">Jawaharlal Nehru</a> in <a href="/wiki/Indian_general_election,_1957" title="Indian general election, 1957">1957</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_general_election,_1962" title="Indian general election, 1962">1962</a> to be re-elected to a consecutive five-year term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBusiness_Standard2009_175-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBusiness_Standard2009-175">[167]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Indian_general_election" title="2014 Indian general election">2014 general election</a>, the BJP became the first political party since 1984 to win a majority and govern without the support of other parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[168]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> of India is <a href="/wiki/Narendra_Modi" title="Narendra Modi">Narendra Modi</a>, who was formerly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Minister" title="Chief Minister">Chief Minister</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a>. On 20 July 2017, <a href="/wiki/Ram_Nath_Kovind" title="Ram Nath Kovind">Ram Nath Kovind</a> was elected India’s 14th President and took the oath of office on 25 July 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-177"><a href="#cite_note-177">[169]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[170]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-179"><a href="#cite_note-179">[171]</a></sup></p>
<p>India is a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federation</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary system</a> governed under the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_India" title="Constitution of India">Constitution of India</a>, which serves as the country's supreme legal document. It is a constitutional republic and <a href="/wiki/Representative_democracy" title="Representative democracy">representative democracy</a>, in which "<a href="/wiki/Majority_rule" title="Majority rule">majority rule</a> is tempered by <a href="/wiki/Minority_rights" title="Minority rights">minority rights</a> protected by <a href="/wiki/Law_of_India" title="Law of India">law</a>". <a href="/wiki/Federalism#India" title="Federalism">Federalism in India</a> defines the power distribution between the Union, or Central, government and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_India" title="States and territories of India">states</a>. The government abides by constitutional <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_powers" title="Separation of powers">checks and balances</a>. The Constitution of India, which came into effect on 26 January 1950,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPylee2003a4_180-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPylee2003a4-180">[172]</a></sup> states in its <a href="/wiki/Preamble_to_the_Constitution_of_India" title="Preamble to the Constitution of India">preamble</a> that India is a <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereign</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialism_in_India" title="Socialism in India">socialist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secular</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">democratic</a> <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDutt1998421_181-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDutt1998421-181">[173]</a></sup> India's form of government, traditionally described as "quasi-federal" with a strong centre and weak states,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWheare198028_182-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWheare198028-182">[174]</a></sup> has grown increasingly federal since the late 1990s as a result of political, economic, and social changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEcheverri-Gent200219–20_183-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEcheverri-Gent200219–20-183">[175]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESinha200425_184-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESinha200425-184">[176]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Union government comprises three branches:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-186"><a href="#cite_note-186">[178]</a></sup></p>
<p>India is a federation composed of 29 states and 7 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Territory" title="Union Territory">union territories</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004_201-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004-201">[193]</a></sup> All states, as well as the union territories of <a href="/wiki/Puducherry" title="Puducherry">Puducherry</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Capital_Territory_of_Delhi" title="National Capital Territory of Delhi">National Capital Territory of Delhi</a>, have elected legislatures and governments, both patterned on the <a href="/wiki/Westminster_system" title="Westminster system">Westminster</a> model. The remaining five union territories are directly ruled by the centre through appointed administrators. In 1956, under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States_Reorganisation_Act" title="States Reorganisation Act">States Reorganisation Act</a>, states were reorganised on a linguistic basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESharma200749_202-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESharma200749-202">[194]</a></sup> Since then, their structure has remained largely unchanged. Each state or union territory is further divided into administrative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Districts_of_India" title="Districts of India">districts</a>. The districts in turn are further divided into <a href="/wiki/Tehsil" title="Tehsil">tehsils</a> and ultimately into villages.</p>
<p>Since its independence in 1947, India has maintained cordial relations with most nations. In the 1950s, it strongly supported decolonisation in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/India_and_the_Non-Aligned_Movement" title="India and the Non-Aligned Movement">played a lead role</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement" title="Non-Aligned Movement">Non-Aligned Movement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERothermund200048,_227_203-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERothermund200048,_227-203">[195]</a></sup> In the late 1980s, the Indian military twice intervened abroad at the invitation of neighbouring countries: a <a href="/wiki/Indian_Peace_Keeping_Force" title="Indian Peace Keeping Force">peace-keeping operation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> between 1987 and 1990; and an armed intervention to prevent a <a href="/wiki/1988_Maldives_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="1988 Maldives coup d'état">1988 coup d'état attempt</a> in Maldives. India has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_relations" title="Indo-Pakistani relations">tense relations</a> with neighbouring Pakistan; the two nations <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_wars_and_conflicts" title="Indo-Pakistani wars and conflicts">have gone to war four times</a>: in <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1947" title="Indo-Pakistani War of 1947">1947</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1965" title="Indo-Pakistani War of 1965">1965</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1971" title="Indo-Pakistani War of 1971">1971</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kargil_War" title="Kargil War">1999</a>. Three of these wars were fought over the <a href="/wiki/Kashmir_conflict" title="Kashmir conflict">disputed territory of Kashmir</a>, while the fourth, the 1971 war, followed from India's support for the <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh_Liberation_War" title="Bangladesh Liberation War">independence of Bangladesh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGilbert2002486–487_204-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGilbert2002486–487-204">[196]</a></sup> After waging the 1962 <a href="/wiki/Sino-Indian_War" title="Sino-Indian War">Sino-Indian War</a> and the 1965 war with Pakistan, India pursued close military and economic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/India-Soviet_Union_relations" title="India-Soviet Union relations">ties with the Soviet Union</a>; by the late 1960s, the Soviet Union was its largest arms supplier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESharma199956_205-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESharma199956-205">[197]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aside from ongoing strategic <a href="/wiki/India%E2%80%93Russia_relations" title="India–Russia relations">relations with Russia</a>, India has wide-ranging <a href="/wiki/India%E2%80%93Israel_relations" title="India–Israel relations">defence relations with Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/France%E2%80%93India_relations" title="France–India relations">France</a>. In recent years, it has played key roles in the <a href="/wiki/South_Asian_Association_for_Regional_Cooperation" title="South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation">South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organisation" title="World Trade Organisation">World Trade Organisation</a>. The nation has provided 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Indian_Armed_Forces" title="Indian Armed Forces">military</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_India" title="Law enforcement in India">police</a> personnel to serve in 35 UN peacekeeping operations across four continents. It participates in the <a href="/wiki/East_Asia_Summit" title="East Asia Summit">East Asia Summit</a>, the <a href="/wiki/G8%2B5" title="G8+5">G8+5</a>, and other multilateral forums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlford2008_206-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlford2008-206">[198]</a></sup> India has close economic ties with <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-207"><a href="#cite_note-207">[199]</a></sup> Asia, and Africa; it pursues a <a href="/wiki/Look_East_policy_(India)" title="Look East policy (India)">"Look East" policy</a> that seeks to strengthen partnerships with the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Southeast_Asian_Nations" title="Association of Southeast Asian Nations">ASEAN</a> nations, <a href="/wiki/India%E2%80%93Japan_relations" title="India–Japan relations">Japan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India%E2%80%93South_Korea_relations" title="India–South Korea relations">South Korea</a> that revolve around many issues, but especially those involving economic investment and regional security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGhosh2009282–289_208-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGhosh2009282–289-208">[200]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESisodiaNaidu20051–8_209-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESisodiaNaidu20051–8-209">[201]</a></sup></p>
<p>China's <a href="/wiki/596_(nuclear_test)" title="596 (nuclear test)">nuclear test of 1964</a>, as well as its repeated threats to intervene in support of Pakistan in the 1965 war, convinced India to develop nuclear weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPerkovich200160–86,_106–125_210-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPerkovich200160–86,_106–125-210">[202]</a></sup> India conducted its <a href="/wiki/Smiling_Buddha" title="Smiling Buddha">first nuclear weapons test</a> in 1974 and carried out <a href="/wiki/Pokhran-II" title="Pokhran-II">further underground testing</a> in 1998. Despite criticism and military sanctions, India has signed neither the <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Nuclear-Test-Ban_Treaty" title="Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty">Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty</a> nor the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty</a>, considering both to be flawed and discriminatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKumar2010_211-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKumar2010-211">[203]</a></sup> India maintains a "<a href="/wiki/No_first_use" title="No first use">no first use</a>" nuclear policy and is developing a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_triad" title="Nuclear triad">nuclear triad</a> capability as a part of its "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minimum_Credible_Deterrence" title="Minimum Credible Deterrence">minimum credible deterrence</a>" doctrine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENair2007_212-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENair2007-212">[204]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPandit2009_213-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPandit2009-213">[205]</a></sup> It is developing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Ballistic_Missile_Defense_Program" title="Indian Ballistic Missile Defense Program">ballistic missile defence shield</a> and, in collaboration with Russia, a <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi/HAL_FGFA" title="Sukhoi/HAL FGFA">fifth-generation fighter jet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Hindu_2011_214-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Hindu_2011-214">[206]</a></sup> Other indigenous military projects involve the design and implementation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vikrant_class_aircraft_carrier" title="Vikrant class aircraft carrier"><i>Vikrant</i>-class aircraft carriers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arihant_class_submarine" title="Arihant class submarine"><i>Arihant</i>-class nuclear submarines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Hindu_2011_214-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Hindu_2011-214">[206]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, India has increased its economic, strategic, and military co-operation with the <a href="/wiki/India%E2%80%93United_States_relations" title="India–United States relations">United States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/India%E2%80%93European_Union_relations" title="India–European Union relations">European Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEuropa_2008_215-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEuropa_2008-215">[207]</a></sup> In 2008, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S.%E2%80%93India_Civil_Nuclear_Agreement" title="U.S.–India Civil Nuclear Agreement">civilian nuclear agreement</a> was signed between India and the United States. Although India possessed nuclear weapons at the time and was not party to the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, it received waivers from the <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Suppliers_Group" title="Nuclear Suppliers Group">Nuclear Suppliers Group</a>, ending earlier restrictions on India's nuclear technology and commerce. As a consequence, India became the sixth <i>de facto</i> nuclear weapons state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Times_of_India_2008_216-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Times_of_India_2008-216">[208]</a></sup> India subsequently signed co-operation agreements involving <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_India" title="Nuclear power in India">civilian nuclear energy</a> with Russia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBritish_Broadcasting_Corporation_2009_217-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBritish_Broadcasting_Corporation_2009-217">[209]</a></sup> France,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERediff_2008_a_218-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERediff_2008_a-218">[210]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/India%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations" title="India–United Kingdom relations">United Kingdom</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReuters2010_219-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReuters2010-219">[211]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93India_relations" title="Canada–India relations">Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECurry2010_220-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECurry2010-220">[212]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_India" title="President of India">President of India</a> is the supreme commander of the nation's armed forces; with 1.395 million active troops, they compose the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_number_of_troops" title="List of countries by number of troops">world's second-largest military</a>. It comprises the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Army" title="Indian Army">Indian Army</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Navy" title="Indian Navy">Indian Navy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Air_Force" title="Indian Air Force">Indian Air Force</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Coast_Guard" title="Indian Coast Guard">Indian Coast Guard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECentral_Intelligence_Agency_221-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECentral_Intelligence_Agency-221">[213]</a></sup> The official Indian <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_military_expenditures" title="List of countries by military expenditures">defence budget</a> for 2011 was US$36.03 billion, or 1.83% of GDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBehera2011_222-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBehera2011-222">[214]</a></sup> For the fiscal year spanning 2012–2013, US$40.44 billion was budgeted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBehera2012_223-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBehera2012-223">[215]</a></sup> According to a 2008 <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_International_Peace_Research_Institute" title="Stockholm International Peace Research Institute">SIPRI</a> report, India's annual military expenditure in terms of purchasing power stood at US$72.7 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStockholm_International_Peace_Research_Institute_2008178_224-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStockholm_International_Peace_Research_Institute_2008178-224">[216]</a></sup> In 2011, the annual defence budget increased by 11.6%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiglani2011_225-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiglani2011-225">[217]</a></sup> although this does not include funds that reach the military through other branches of government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShukla2011_226-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShukla2011-226">[218]</a></sup> As of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=India&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, India is the world's largest arms importer; between 2007 and 2011, it accounted for 10% of funds spent on international arms purchases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStockholm_International_Peace_Research_Initiative_2012_227-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStockholm_International_Peace_Research_Initiative_2012-227">[219]</a></sup> Much of the military expenditure was focused on defence against Pakistan and countering growing Chinese influence in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiglani2011_225-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiglani2011-225">[217]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> (IMF), the Indian economy in 2017 was nominally worth US$2.454 trillion; it is the sixth-largest economy by market exchange rates, and is, at US$9.489 trillion, the third-largest by <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">purchasing power parity</a>, or PPP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imf2_20-5"><a href="#cite_note-imf2-20">[16]</a></sup> With its average annual GDP growth rate of 5.8% over the past two decades, and reaching 6.1% during 2011–12,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInternational_Monetary_Fund_20112_228-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInternational_Monetary_Fund_20112-228">[220]</a></sup> India is one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_real_GDP_growth_rate" title="List of countries by real GDP growth rate">world's fastest-growing economies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENayakGoldarAgrawal2010xxv_229-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENayakGoldarAgrawal2010xxv-229">[221]</a></sup> However, the country ranks 140th in the world in <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(nominal)_per_capita" title="List of countries by GDP (nominal) per capita">nominal GDP per capita</a> and 129th in <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)_per_capita" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP) per capita">GDP per capita at PPP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInternational_Monetary_Fund_230-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInternational_Monetary_Fund-230">[222]</a></sup> Until 1991, all Indian governments followed <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionist</a> policies that were influenced by socialist economics. Widespread <a href="/wiki/Licence_Raj" title="Licence Raj">state intervention and regulation</a> largely walled the economy off from the outside world. An acute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1991_India_economic_crisis" title="1991 India economic crisis">balance of payments crisis in 1991</a> forced the nation to <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalisation_in_India" title="Economic liberalisation in India">liberalise its economy</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolpert2003xiv_231-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolpert2003xiv-231">[223]</a></sup> since then it has slowly moved towards a free-market system<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrganisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development_2007_232-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrganisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development_2007-232">[224]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGargan1992_233-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGargan1992-233">[225]</a></sup> by emphasising both foreign trade and direct investment inflows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlamgir200823,_97_234-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlamgir200823,_97-234">[226]</a></sup> India has been a member of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WTO" title="WTO">WTO</a> since 1 January 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWTO_1995_235-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWTO_1995-235">[227]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 513.7-million-worker <a href="/wiki/Labour_in_India" title="Labour in India">Indian labour force</a> is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_labour_force" title="List of countries by labour force">world's second-largest</a>, as of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=India&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECentral_Intelligence_Agency_221-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECentral_Intelligence_Agency-221">[213]</a></sup> The service sector makes up 55.6% of GDP, the industrial sector 26.3% and the agricultural sector 18.1%. India's foreign exchange remittances of US$70 billion in 2014, the largest in the world, contributed to its economy by 25 million Indians working in foreign countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-236"><a href="#cite_note-236">[228]</a></sup> Major agricultural products include rice, wheat, oilseed, cotton, jute, tea, sugarcane, and potatoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004_201-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004-201">[193]</a></sup> Major industries include textiles, telecommunications, chemicals, pharmaceuticals, biotechnology, food processing, steel, transport equipment, cement, mining, petroleum, machinery, and software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004_201-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004-201">[193]</a></sup> In 2006, the share of external trade in India's GDP stood at 24%, up from 6% in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrganisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development_2007_232-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrganisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development_2007-232">[224]</a></sup> In 2008, India's share of world trade was 1.68%;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Times_of_India_2009_237-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Times_of_India_2009-237">[229]</a></sup> In 2011, India was the world's <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_imports" title="List of countries by imports">tenth-largest importer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_exports" title="List of countries by exports">nineteenth-largest exporter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Trade_Organisation_2010_238-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Trade_Organisation_2010-238">[230]</a></sup> Major exports include petroleum products, textile goods, jewellery, software, engineering goods, chemicals, and leather manufactures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004_201-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004-201">[193]</a></sup> Major imports include crude oil, machinery, gems, fertiliser, and chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004_201-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELibrary_of_Congress2004-201">[193]</a></sup> Between 2001 and 2011, the contribution of petrochemical and engineering goods to total exports grew from 14% to 42%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEconomist_2011_239-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEconomist_2011-239">[231]</a></sup> India was the second largest textile exporter after China in the world in the calendar year 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-240"><a href="#cite_note-240">[232]</a></sup></p>
<p>Averaging an economic growth rate of 7.5% for several years prior to 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrganisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development_2007_232-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrganisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development_2007-232">[224]</a></sup> India has more than doubled its hourly wage rates during the first decade of the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBonner2010_241-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBonner2010-241">[233]</a></sup> Some 431 million Indians have left poverty since 1985; India's middle classes are projected to number around 580 million by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarrellBeinhocker2007_242-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarrellBeinhocker2007-242">[234]</a></sup> Though ranking 51st in <a href="/wiki/Global_Competitiveness_Report" title="Global Competitiveness Report">global competitiveness</a>, India ranks 17th in financial market sophistication, 24th in the banking sector, 44th in business sophistication, and 39th in innovation, ahead of several advanced economies, as of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=India&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchwab2010_243-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchwab2010-243">[235]</a></sup> With 7 of the world's top 15 information technology outsourcing companies based in India, the country is viewed as the second-most favourable outsourcing destination after the United States, as of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=India&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheth2009_244-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheth2009-244">[236]</a></sup> India's consumer market, the world's <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_consumer_markets" title="List of largest consumer markets">eleventh-largest</a>, is expected to become fifth-largest by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarrellBeinhocker2007_242-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarrellBeinhocker2007-242">[234]</a></sup> However, hardly 2% of Indians pay <a href="/wiki/Income_tax" title="Income tax">income taxes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-245"><a href="#cite_note-245">[237]</a></sup></p>
<p>Driven by growth, India's nominal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP_per_capita" title="GDP per capita">GDP per capita</a> has steadily increased from US$329 in 1991, when economic liberalisation began, to US$1,265 in 2010, to an estimated US$1,723 in 2016, and is expected to grow to US$2,358 by 2020;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imf2_20-6"><a href="#cite_note-imf2-20">[16]</a></sup> however, it has remained lower than those of other Asian developing countries such as Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines, Sri Lanka, and Thailand, and is expected to remain so in the near future. However, it is higher than Pakistan, Nepal, Afghanistan, Bangladesh and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInternational_Monetary_Fund_2011_246-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInternational_Monetary_Fund_2011-246">[238]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a 2011 <a href="/wiki/PricewaterhouseCoopers" title="PricewaterhouseCoopers">PricewaterhouseCoopers</a> report, India's GDP at purchasing power parity could overtake that of the United States by 2045.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPricewaterhouseCoopers2011_247-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPricewaterhouseCoopers2011-247">[239]</a></sup> During the next four decades, Indian GDP is expected to grow at an annualised average of 8%, making it potentially the world's fastest-growing major economy until 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPricewaterhouseCoopers2011_247-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPricewaterhouseCoopers2011-247">[239]</a></sup> The report highlights key growth factors: a young and rapidly growing working-age population; growth in the manufacturing sector because of rising education and engineering skill levels; and sustained growth of the consumer market driven by a rapidly growing middle-class. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPricewaterhouseCoopers2011_247-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPricewaterhouseCoopers2011-247">[239]</a></sup> The World Bank cautions that, for India to achieve its economic potential, it must continue to focus on public sector reform, <a href="/wiki/Transport_in_India" title="Transport in India">transport infrastructure</a>, agricultural and rural development, removal of labour regulations, <a href="/wiki/Education_in_India" title="Education in India">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_India" title="Energy policy of India">energy security</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_India" title="Healthcare in India">public health</a> and nutrition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Bank_2010_248-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Bank_2010-248">[240]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Worldwide Cost of Living Report 2017 released by the <a href="/wiki/Economist_Intelligence_Unit" title="Economist Intelligence Unit">Economist Intelligence Unit</a> (EIU) which was created by comparing more than 400 individual prices across 160 products and services, four of the cheapest cities were in India: <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">Bangalore</a> (3rd), <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a> (5th), <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a> (5th) and <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a> (8th).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-249"><a href="#cite_note-249">[241]</a></sup></p>
<p>India's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communications_in_India" title="Communications in India">telecommunication industry</a>, the world's fastest-growing, added 227 million subscribers during the period 2010–11,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETelecom_Regulatory_Authority_2011_250-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETelecom_Regulatory_Authority_2011-250">[242]</a></sup> and after the third quarter of 2017, India surpassed the US to become the second largest smartphone market in the world after China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-251"><a href="#cite_note-251">[243]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry_in_India" title="Automotive industry in India">Indian automotive industry</a>, the world's second fastest growing, increased domestic sales by 26% during 2009–10,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBusiness_Line_2010_252-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBusiness_Line_2010-252">[244]</a></sup> and exports by 36% during 2008–09.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEExpress_India_2009_253-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEExpress_India_2009-253">[245]</a></sup> India's capacity to generate electrical power is 300 gigawatts, of which 42 gigawatts is <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_India" title="Renewable energy in India">renewable</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Par_254-0"><a href="#cite_note-Par-254">[246]</a></sup> At the end of 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Information_technology_in_India" title="Information technology in India">Indian IT industry</a> employed 2.8 million professionals, generated revenues close to US$100 billion equalling 7.5% of Indian GDP and contributed 26% of India's merchandise exports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENasscom_2011–2012_255-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENasscom_2011–2012-255">[247]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry_in_India" title="Pharmaceutical industry in India">pharmaceutical industry in India</a> is among the significant emerging markets for the global pharmaceutical industry. The Indian pharmaceutical market is expected to reach $48.5 billion by 2020. India's R &amp; D spending constitutes 60% of the <a href="/wiki/Biopharmaceutical" title="Biopharmaceutical">biopharmaceutical</a> industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-256"><a href="#cite_note-256">[248]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-257"><a href="#cite_note-257">[249]</a></sup> India is among the top 12 biotech destinations of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-258"><a href="#cite_note-258">[250]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYep2011_259-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYep2011-259">[251]</a></sup> The Indian biotech industry grew by 15.1% in 2012–13, increasing its revenues from 204.4 billion INR (Indian rupees) to 235.24 billion INR (3.94 B US$ – exchange rate June 2013: 1 US$ approx. 60 INR).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-260"><a href="#cite_note-260">[252]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite economic growth during recent decades, India continues to face socio-economic challenges. In 2006, India contained the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_in_India" title="Poverty in India">largest number</a> of people living below the World Bank's international poverty line of US$1.25 per day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Bank_2006_261-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Bank_2006-261">[253]</a></sup> the proportion having decreased from 60% in 1981 to 42% in 2005;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Bank_a_262-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Bank_a-262">[254]</a></sup> under its later revised poverty line, it was 21% in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-264"><a href="#cite_note-264">[i]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WB2015_265-0"><a href="#cite_note-WB2015-265">[256]</a></sup> 30.7% of India's children under the age of five are underweight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-266"><a href="#cite_note-266">[257]</a></sup> According to a <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> report in 2015, 15% of the population is undernourished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-267"><a href="#cite_note-267">[258]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-268"><a href="#cite_note-268">[259]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Day_Meal_Scheme" title="Mid-Day Meal Scheme">Mid-Day Meal Scheme</a> attempts to lower these rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDrèzeGoyal200846_269-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDrèzeGoyal200846-269">[260]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a Walk Free Foundation report in 2016, there were 18.3 million people in India living in the forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_slavery" title="Modern slavery">modern slavery</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonded_labour" title="Bonded labour">bonded labour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Child_labour" title="Child labour">child labour</a>, human trafficking, forced begging, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-270"><a href="#cite_note-270">[261]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-271"><a href="#cite_note-271">[262]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-272"><a href="#cite_note-272">[263]</a></sup> According to 2011 census, there were 10.1 million child labourers in the country, a decline of 2.6 million from 12.6 million child labourers in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-273"><a href="#cite_note-273">[264]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1991, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Indian_states_by_GDP" title="List of Indian states by GDP">economic inequality</a> between India's states has consistently grown: the per-capita <a href="/wiki/Net_domestic_product" title="Net domestic product">net state domestic product</a> of the richest states in 2007 was 3.2 times that of the poorest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPalGhosh2007_274-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPalGhosh2007-274">[265]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Corruption_in_India" title="Corruption in India">Corruption in India</a> is perceived to have decreased. According to <a href="/wiki/Corruption_Perceptions_Index" title="Corruption Perceptions Index">Corruption Perceptions Index</a>, India ranked 76th out of 176 countries in 2016, from 85th in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-275"><a href="#cite_note-275">[266]</a></sup></p>
<p>With 1,210,193,422 residents reported in the <a href="/wiki/2011_Census_of_India" title="2011 Census of India">2011 provisional census report</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011160_276-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011160-276">[267]</a></sup> India is the world's second-most populous country. Its population grew by 17.64% during 2001–2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011165_277-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011165-277">[268]</a></sup> compared to 21.54% growth in the previous decade (1991–2001).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011165_277-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011165-277">[268]</a></sup> The human sex ratio, according to the 2011 census, is 940 females per 1,000 males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011160_276-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011160-276">[267]</a></sup> The median age was 27.6 as of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=India&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECentral_Intelligence_Agency_221-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECentral_Intelligence_Agency-221">[213]</a></sup> The first post-colonial census, conducted in 1951, counted 361.1 million people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-278"><a href="#cite_note-278">[269]</a></sup> Medical advances made in the last 50 years as well as increased agricultural productivity brought about by the "<a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution_in_India" title="Green Revolution in India">Green Revolution</a>" have caused India's population to grow rapidly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERorabacher201035–39_279-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERorabacher201035–39-279">[270]</a></sup> India continues to face several public health-related challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Health_Organisation_2006_280-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Health_Organisation_2006-280">[271]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoston_Analytics2009_281-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoston_Analytics2009-281">[272]</a></sup></p>
<p>Life expectancy in India is at 68 years, with life expectancy for women being 69.6 years and for men being 67.3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-282"><a href="#cite_note-282">[273]</a></sup> There are around 50 physicians per 100,000 Indians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDevRao2009329_283-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevRao2009329-283">[274]</a></sup> The number of Indians living in urban areas has grown by 31.2% between 1991 and 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGarg2005_284-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGarg2005-284">[275]</a></sup> Yet, in 2001, over 70% lived in rural areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDysonVisaria2005115–129_285-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDysonVisaria2005115–129-285">[276]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERatna2007271–272_286-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERatna2007271–272-286">[277]</a></sup> The level of urbanisation increased from 27.81% in 2001 Census to 31.16% in 2011 Census. The slowing down of the overall growth rate of population was due to the sharp decline in the growth rate in rural areas since 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChandramouli2011_287-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChandramouli2011-287">[278]</a></sup> According to the 2011 census, there are 53 <a href="/wiki/List_of_million-plus_urban_agglomerations_in_India" title="List of million-plus urban agglomerations in India">million-plus urban agglomerations in India</a>; among them <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">Bangalore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyderabad" title="Hyderabad">Hyderabad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ahmedabad" title="Ahmedabad">Ahmedabad</a>, in decreasing order by population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-censusindia_2011_288-0"><a href="#cite_note-censusindia_2011-288">[279]</a></sup> The literacy rate in 2011 was 74.04%: 65.46% among females and 82.14% among males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011163_289-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011163-289">[280]</a></sup> The rural urban literacy gap which was 21.2 percentage points in 2001, dropped to 16.1 percentage points in 2011. The improvement in literacy rate in rural area is two times that in urban areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChandramouli2011_287-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChandramouli2011-287">[278]</a></sup> Kerala is the most literate state with 93.91% literacy; while Bihar the least with 63.82%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011163_289-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProvisional_Population_Totals,_Census2011163-289">[280]</a></sup></p>
<p>India is home to <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">two major language families</a>: <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indo-Aryan</a> (spoken by about 74% of the population) and <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">Dravidian</a> (spoken by 24% of the population). Other languages spoken in India come from the <a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" title="Austroasiatic languages">Austroasiatic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan</a> language families. India has no national language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDharwadker2010168–194,_186_290-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDharwadker2010168–194,_186-290">[281]</a></sup> Hindi, with the largest number of speakers, is the official language of the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOttenheimer2008303_291-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOttenheimer2008303-291">[282]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMallikarjun2004_292-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMallikarjun2004-292">[283]</a></sup> English is used extensively in business and administration and has the status of a "subsidiary official language";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Home_Affairs_1960_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMinistry_of_Home_Affairs_1960-5">[4]</a></sup> it is important in <a href="/wiki/Education_in_India" title="Education in India">education</a>, especially as a medium of higher education. Each state and union territory has one or more official languages, and the constitution recognises in particular 22 "scheduled languages". The Constitution of India recognises 212 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scheduled_castes_and_scheduled_tribes" title="Scheduled castes and scheduled tribes">scheduled</a> tribal groups which together constitute about 7.5% of the country's population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBonner199081_293-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBonner199081-293">[284]</a></sup> The 2011 census reported that the <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_India" title="Religion in India">religion in India</a> with the largest number of followers was <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_India" title="Hinduism in India">Hinduism</a> (79.80% of the population), followed by <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_India" title="Islam in India">Islam</a> (14.23%); the remaining were <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_India" title="Christianity in India">Christianity</a> (2.30%), <a href="/wiki/Sikhism_in_India" title="Sikhism in India">Sikhism</a> (1.72%), <a href="/wiki/History_of_Buddhism_in_India" title="History of Buddhism in India">Buddhism</a> (0.70%), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistics_of_Jainism" title="Statistics of Jainism">Jainism</a> (0.36%) and others<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remaining_religions_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-remaining_religions-13">[c]</a></sup> (0.9%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census2011religion_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Census2011religion-14">[11]</a></sup> India has the world's largest Hindu, Sikh, Jain, <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism_in_India" title="Zoroastrianism in India">Zoroastrian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith_in_India" title="Bahá'í Faith in India">Bahá'í populations</a>, and has the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_Muslim_population#List" title="List of countries by Muslim population">third-largest</a> Muslim population—the largest for a non-Muslim majority country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-294"><a href="#cite_note-294">[285]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-295"><a href="#cite_note-295">[286]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indian cultural history spans more than 4,500 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKuiper201015_296-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKuiper201015-296">[287]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Vedic_period" title="Vedic period">Vedic period</a> (c. 1700 – 500 BCE), the foundations of <a href="/wiki/Hindu_philosophy" title="Hindu philosophy">Hindu philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindu_mythology" title="Hindu mythology">mythology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu_theology" title="Hindu theology">theology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hindu_texts" title="Hindu texts">literature</a> were laid, and many beliefs and practices which still exist today, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">dhárma</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">kárma</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">yóga</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Moksha" title="Moksha">mokṣa</a></i>, were established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKuiper201086_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKuiper201086-29">[22]</a></sup> India is notable for its <a href="/wiki/Indian_religions" title="Indian religions">religious diversity</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a> among the nation's major religions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeehs20022–5_297-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeehs20022–5-297">[288]</a></sup> The predominant religion, Hinduism, has been shaped by various historical schools of thought, including those of the <i><a href="/wiki/Upanishads" title="Upanishads">Upanishads</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDeutsch19693,_78_298-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDeutsch19693,_78-298">[289]</a></sup> the <i><a href="/wiki/Yoga_Sutras_of_Patanjali" title="Yoga Sutras of Patanjali">Yoga Sutras</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Bhakti" title="Bhakti"><i>Bhakti</i> movement</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeehs20022–5_297-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeehs20022–5-297">[288]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_philosophy" title="Buddhist philosophy">Buddhist philosophy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENakamura1999_299-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENakamura1999-299">[290]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of <a href="/wiki/Architecture_of_India" title="Architecture of India">Indian architecture</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Taj_Mahal" title="Taj Mahal">Taj Mahal</a>, other works of <a href="/wiki/Mughal_architecture" title="Mughal architecture">Mughal architecture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_architecture" title="Dravidian architecture">South Indian architecture</a>, blends ancient local traditions with imported styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKuiper2010296–329_300-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKuiper2010296–329-300">[291]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Indian_vernacular_architecture" title="Indian vernacular architecture">Vernacular architecture</a> is also highly regional in it flavours. <i><a href="/wiki/Vastu_shastra" title="Vastu shastra">Vastu shastra</a></i>, literally "science of construction" or "architecture" and ascribed to <a href="/wiki/Mamuni_Mayan" title="Mamuni Mayan">Mamuni Mayan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESilverman200720_301-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESilverman200720-301">[292]</a></sup> explores how the laws of nature affect human dwellings;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKumar20005_302-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKumar20005-302">[293]</a></sup> it employs precise geometry and directional alignments to reflect perceived cosmic constructs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts200473_303-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts200473-303">[294]</a></sup> As applied in <a href="/wiki/Hindu_temple_architecture" title="Hindu temple architecture">Hindu temple architecture</a>, it is influenced by the <i><a href="/wiki/Shilpa_Shastras" title="Shilpa Shastras">Shilpa Shastras</a></i>, a series of foundational texts whose basic mythological form is the <i>Vastu-Purusha mandala</i>, a square that embodied the "<a href="/wiki/Absolute_(philosophy)" title="Absolute (philosophy)">absolute</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangMoleski2010151–152_304-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangMoleski2010151–152-304">[295]</a></sup> The Taj Mahal, built in <a href="/wiki/Agra" title="Agra">Agra</a> between 1631 and 1648 by orders of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Shah_Jahan" title="Shah Jahan">Shah Jahan</a> in memory of his wife, has been described in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_List" title="UNESCO World Heritage List">UNESCO World Heritage List</a> as "the jewel of Muslim art in India and one of the universally admired masterpieces of the world's heritage".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUnited_Nations_Educational,_Scientific,_and_Cultural_Organisation_305-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUnited_Nations_Educational,_Scientific,_and_Cultural_Organisation-305">[296]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Indo-Saracenic_Revival_architecture" title="Indo-Saracenic Revival architecture">Indo-Saracenic Revival architecture</a>, developed by the British in the late 19th century, drew on <a href="/wiki/Indo-Islamic_architecture" title="Indo-Islamic architecture">Indo-Islamic architecture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChopra201146_306-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChopra201146-306">[297]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest literary writings in India, composed between 1700 BCE and 1200 CE, were in the Sanskrit language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoibergRamchandani2000_307-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoibergRamchandani2000-307">[298]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESarma2009_308-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESarma2009-308">[299]</a></sup> Prominent works of this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_literature" title="Sanskrit literature">Sanskrit literature</a> include <a href="/wiki/Indian_epic_poetry" title="Indian epic poetry">epics</a> such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahābhārata</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a></i>, the dramas of <a href="/wiki/K%C4%81lid%C4%81sa" title="Kālidāsa">Kālidāsa</a> such as the <i>Abhijñānaśākuntalam</i> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Recognition_of_%C5%9Aakuntal%C4%81" title="The Recognition of Śakuntalā">The Recognition of Śakuntalā</a></i>), and poetry such as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_Classical_poetry" title="Sanskrit Classical poetry">Mahākāvya</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohnson2008_309-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson2008-309">[300]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacDonell20041–40_310-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacDonell20041–40-310">[301]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKālidāsaJohnson2001_311-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKālidāsaJohnson2001-311">[302]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamasutra" title="Kamasutra">Kamasutra</a>, the famous book about <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a> also originated in India. Developed between 600 BCE and 300 CE in South India, the <a href="/wiki/Sangam_literature" title="Sangam literature"><i>Sangam</i> literature</a>, consisting of 2,381 poems, is regarded as a predecessor of <a href="/wiki/Tamil_literature" title="Tamil literature">Tamil literature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZvelebil199712_312-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZvelebil199712-312">[303]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHart1975_313-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHart1975-313">[304]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEncyclopædia_Britannica2008_314-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEncyclopædia_Britannica2008-314">[305]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERamanujan1985ix–x_315-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERamanujan1985ix–x-315">[306]</a></sup> From the 14th to the 18th centuries, India's literary traditions went through a period of drastic change because of the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Bhakti_movement" title="Bhakti movement">devotional poets</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Kabir" title="Kabir">Kabīr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tulsidas" title="Tulsidas">Tulsīdās</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guru_Nanak" title="Guru Nanak">Guru Nānak</a>. This period was characterised by a varied and wide spectrum of thought and expression; as a consequence, medieval Indian literary works differed significantly from classical traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDas2005_316-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDas2005-316">[307]</a></sup> In the 19th century, Indian writers took a new interest in social questions and psychological descriptions. In the 20th century, Indian literature was influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Works_of_Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Works of Rabindranath Tagore">works</a> of Bengali poet and novelist <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">Rabindranath Tagore</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDatta2006_317-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDatta2006-317">[308]</a></sup> who was a recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Music_of_India" title="Music of India">Indian music</a> ranges over various traditions and regional styles. <a href="/wiki/Indian_classical_music" title="Indian classical music">Classical music</a> encompasses two genres and their various folk offshoots: the northern <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_classical_music" title="Hindustani classical music">Hindustani</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Carnatic_music" title="Carnatic music">Carnatic</a> schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMasseyMassey1998_318-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMasseyMassey1998-318">[309]</a></sup> Regionalised popular forms include <a href="/wiki/Filmi" title="Filmi">filmi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_folk_music" title="Indian folk music">folk music</a>; the syncretic tradition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Baul" title="Baul">bauls</a></i> is a well-known form of the latter. <a href="/wiki/Dance_in_India" title="Dance in India">Indian dance</a> also features diverse folk and classical forms. Among the better-known <a href="/wiki/List_of_Indian_folk_dances" title="List of Indian folk dances">folk dances</a> are the <i><a href="/wiki/Bhangra_(dance)" title="Bhangra (dance)">bhangra</a></i> of Punjab, the <i><a href="/wiki/Bihu_dance" title="Bihu dance">bihu</a></i> of Assam, the <i><a href="/wiki/Chhau_dance" title="Chhau dance">chhau</a></i> of Odisha, West Bengal and Jharkhand, <i><a href="/wiki/Garba_(dance)" title="Garba (dance)">garba</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dandiya_Raas" title="Dandiya Raas">dandiya</a></i> of Gujarat, <i><a href="/wiki/Ghoomar" title="Ghoomar">ghoomar</a></i> of Rajasthan, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Lavani" title="Lavani">lavani</a></i> of Maharashtra. Eight dance forms, many with narrative forms and mythological elements, have been accorded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Indian_dance" title="Classical Indian dance">classical dance status</a> by India's <a href="/wiki/Sangeet_Natak_Akademi" title="Sangeet Natak Akademi">National Academy of Music, Dance, and Drama</a>. These are: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bharata_Natyam" title="Bharata Natyam">bharatanatyam</a></i> of the state of Tamil Nadu, <i><a href="/wiki/Kathak" title="Kathak">kathak</a></i> of Uttar Pradesh, <i><a href="/wiki/Kathakali" title="Kathakali">kathakali</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mohiniyattam" title="Mohiniyattam">mohiniyattam</a></i> of Kerala, <i><a href="/wiki/Kuchipudi" title="Kuchipudi">kuchipudi</a></i> of Andhra Pradesh, <i><a href="/wiki/Manipuri_dance" title="Manipuri dance">manipuri</a></i> of Manipur, <i><a href="/wiki/Odissi" title="Odissi">odissi</a></i> of Odisha, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Sattriya" title="Sattriya">sattriya</a></i> of Assam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEncyclopædia_Britannica_b_319-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEncyclopædia_Britannica_b-319">[310]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theatre_in_India" title="Theatre in India">Theatre in India</a> melds music, dance, and improvised or written dialogue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELal200423,_30,_235_320-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELal200423,_30,_235-320">[311]</a></sup> Often based on Hindu mythology, but also borrowing from medieval romances or social and political events, Indian theatre includes the <i><a href="/wiki/Bhavai" title="Bhavai">bhavai</a></i> of Gujarat, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jatra_(Bengal)" title="Jatra (Bengal)">jatra</a></i> of West Bengal, the <i><a href="/wiki/Nautanki" title="Nautanki">nautanki</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ramlila" title="Ramlila">ramlila</a></i> of North India, <i><a href="/wiki/Tamasha" title="Tamasha">tamasha</a></i> of Maharashtra, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burrakatha" title="Burrakatha">burrakatha</a></i> of Andhra Pradesh, <i><a href="/wiki/Terukkuttu" title="Terukkuttu">terukkuttu</a></i> of Tamil Nadu, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Yakshagana" title="Yakshagana">yakshagana</a></i> of Karnataka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKaranth200226_321-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKaranth200226-321">[312]</a></sup> India has a theatre training institute <a href="/wiki/National_School_of_Drama" title="National School of Drama">N.S.D</a> that is situated at <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a> It is an autonomous organisation under the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Culture_(India)" title="Ministry of Culture (India)">Ministry of Culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_India" title="Government of India">Government of India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-322"><a href="#cite_note-322">[313]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_India" title="Cinema of India">Indian film industry</a> produces the world's most-watched cinema.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDissanayakeGokulsing2004_323-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDissanayakeGokulsing2004-323">[314]</a></sup> Established regional cinematic traditions exist in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Assam" title="Cinema of Assam">Assamese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_West_Bengal" title="Cinema of West Bengal">Bengali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhojpuri_cinema" title="Bhojpuri cinema">Bhojpuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">Hindi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Karnataka" title="Cinema of Karnataka">Kannada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malayalam_cinema" title="Malayalam cinema">Malayalam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Punjab" title="Cinema of Punjab">Punjabi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_cinema" title="Gujarati cinema">Gujarati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marathi_cinema" title="Marathi cinema">Marathi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Odisha" title="Cinema of Odisha">Odia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_cinema" title="Tamil cinema">Tamil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Telugu_cinema" title="Telugu cinema">Telugu</a> languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERajadhyakshaWillemen1999652_324-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERajadhyakshaWillemen1999652-324">[315]</a></sup> South Indian cinema attracts more than 75% of national film revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Economic_Times_325-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Economic_Times-325">[316]</a></sup></p>
<p>Television broadcasting began in India in 1959 as a state-run medium of communication and had slow expansion for more than two decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-326"><a href="#cite_note-326">[317]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKaminskyLong2011684–692_327-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKaminskyLong2011684–692-327">[318]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Doordarshan" title="Doordarshan">state monopoly</a> on television broadcast ended in the 1990s and, since then, satellite channels have increasingly shaped popular culture of Indian society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMehta20081–10_328-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMehta20081–10-328">[319]</a></sup> Today, television is the most penetrative media in India; industry estimates indicate that as of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=India&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> there are over 554 million TV consumers, 462 million with satellite and/or cable connections, compared to other forms of mass media such as press (350 million), radio (156 million) or internet (37 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMedia_Research_Users_Council_2012_329-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMedia_Research_Users_Council_2012-329">[320]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indian cuisine encompasses a wide variety of regional and traditional cuisines, often depending on a particular <a href="/wiki/States_and_union_territories_of_India" title="States and union territories of India">state</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Maharashtrian_cuisine" title="Maharashtrian cuisine">Maharashtrian cuisine</a>). <a href="/wiki/Staple_food" title="Staple food">Staple foods</a> of Indian cuisine include <a href="/wiki/Pearl_millet" title="Pearl millet">pearl millet</a> (<i>bājra</i>), <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whole-wheat_flour" title="Whole-wheat flour">whole-wheat flour</a> (<i>aṭṭa</i>), and a variety of <a href="/wiki/Lentil" title="Lentil">lentils</a>, such as <i>masoor</i> (most often red <a href="/wiki/Lentil" title="Lentil">lentils</a>), <i>toor</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pigeon_pea" title="Pigeon pea">pigeon peas</a>), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urad_(bean)" title="Urad (bean)">urad</a></i> (black gram), and <i>mong</i> (<a href="/wiki/Mung_bean" title="Mung bean">mung beans</a>). Lentils may be used whole, dehusked—for example, <i>dhuli moong</i> or <i>dhuli urad</i>—or split. Split lentils, or <i><a href="/wiki/Dal" title="Dal">dal</a></i>, are used extensively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnston1958_330-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnston1958-330">[321]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spice_trade" title="Spice trade">spice trade</a> between India and Europe is often cited by historians as the primary catalyst for Europe's <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-331"><a href="#cite_note-331">[322]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traditional Indian society is sometimes defined by social hierarchy. The <a href="/wiki/Caste_system_in_India" title="Caste system in India">Indian caste system</a> embodies much of the social stratification and many of the social restrictions found in the Indian subcontinent. Social classes are defined by thousands of endogamous hereditary groups, often termed as <i><a href="/wiki/J%C4%81ti" title="Jāti">jātis</a></i>, or "castes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchwartzberg2011_332-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchwartzberg2011-332">[323]</a></sup> India declared untouchability to be illegal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-333"><a href="#cite_note-333">[324]</a></sup> in 1947 and has since enacted other anti-discriminatory laws and social welfare initiatives. At the workplace in urban India and in international or leading Indian companies, the caste related identification has pretty much lost its importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMessner200951-53_334-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMessner200951-53-334">[325]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMessner201227-28_335-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMessner201227-28-335">[326]</a></sup></p>
<p>Family values are important in the Indian tradition, and multi-generational patriarchal joint families have been the norm in India, though nuclear families are becoming common in urban areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMakar2007_336-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakar2007-336">[327]</a></sup> An overwhelming majority of Indians, with their consent, have <a href="/wiki/Arranged_marriage_in_the_Indian_subcontinent" title="Arranged marriage in the Indian subcontinent">their marriages arranged</a> by their parents or other elders in the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMedora2003_337-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMedora2003-337">[328]</a></sup> Marriage is thought to be for life,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMedora2003_337-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMedora2003-337">[328]</a></sup> and the divorce rate is extremely low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesRamdas2005111_338-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesRamdas2005111-338">[329]</a></sup> As of 2001<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=India&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, just 1.6 percent of <a href="/wiki/Women_in_India" title="Women in India">Indian women</a> were divorced but this figure was rising due to their education and economic independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJonesRamdas2005111_338-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJonesRamdas2005111-338">[329]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Child_marriage" title="Child marriage">Child marriages</a> are common, especially in rural areas; many women wed before reaching 18, which is their legal marriageable age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECullen-Dupont200996_339-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECullen-Dupont200996-339">[330]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Female_infanticide_in_India" title="Female infanticide in India">Female infanticide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Female_foeticide_in_India" title="Female foeticide in India">female foeticide in the country</a> have caused a discrepancy in the sex ratio, as of 2005<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=India&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> it was estimated that there were 50 million more males than females in the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBunting2011_340-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBunting2011-340">[331]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAgnivesh2005_341-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAgnivesh2005-341">[332]</a></sup> However a report from 2011 has shown improvement in the gender ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-342"><a href="#cite_note-342">[333]</a></sup> The payment of <a href="/wiki/Dowry_system_in_India" title="Dowry system in India">dowry</a>, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dowry_law_in_India" title="Dowry law in India">illegal</a>, remains widespread across class lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-343"><a href="#cite_note-343">[334]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dowry_deaths" title="Dowry deaths">Deaths resulting from dowry</a>, mostly from <a href="/wiki/Bride_burning" title="Bride burning">bride burning</a>, are on the rise, despite stringent anti-dowry laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-344"><a href="#cite_note-344">[335]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_India" title="Public holidays in India">Indian festivals</a> are religious in origin. The best known include <a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">Diwali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ganesh_Chaturthi" title="Ganesh Chaturthi">Ganesh Chaturthi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thai_Pongal" title="Thai Pongal">Thai Pongal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holi" title="Holi">Holi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Durga_Puja" title="Durga Puja">Durga Puja</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eid_ul-Fitr" title="Eid ul-Fitr">Eid ul-Fitr</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakr-Id" title="Bakr-Id">Bakr-Id</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_worldwide#India" title="Christmas worldwide">Christmas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vaisakhi" title="Vaisakhi">Vaisakhi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-345"><a href="#cite_note-345">[336]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-346"><a href="#cite_note-346">[337]</a></sup> India has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_days_in_India" title="National days in India">three national holidays</a> which are observed in all states and union territories – <a href="/wiki/Republic_Day_(India)" title="Republic Day (India)">Republic Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(India)" title="Independence Day (India)">Independence Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gandhi_Jayanti" title="Gandhi Jayanti">Gandhi Jayanti</a>. Other sets of holidays, varying between nine and twelve, are officially observed in individual states.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/History_of_cotton" title="History of cotton">Cotton</a> was domesticated in India by 4000 BCE. Traditional <a href="/wiki/Clothing_in_India" title="Clothing in India">Indian dress</a> varies in colour and style across regions and depends on various factors, including climate and faith. Popular styles of dress include draped garments such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Sari" title="Sari">sari</a></i> for women and the <i><a href="/wiki/Dhoti" title="Dhoti">dhoti</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Lungi" title="Lungi">lungi</a></i> for men. Stitched clothes, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Shalwar_kameez" title="Shalwar kameez">shalwar kameez</a></i> for women and <i><a href="/wiki/Kurta" title="Kurta">kurta</a></i>–<i><a href="/wiki/Pajamas" title="Pajamas">pyjama</a></i> combinations or European-style trousers and shirts for men, are also popular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETarlo1996xii,_xii,_11,_15,_28,_46_347-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETarlo1996xii,_xii,_11,_15,_28,_46-347">[338]</a></sup> Use of delicate jewellery, modelled on real flowers worn in ancient India, is part of a tradition dating back some 5,000 years; gemstones are also worn in India as talismans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEraly2008160_348-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEraly2008160-348">[339]</a></sup></p>
<p>In India, several traditional indigenous sports remain fairly popular, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Kabaddi" title="Kabaddi">kabaddi</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kho_kho" title="Kho kho">kho kho</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pehlwani" title="Pehlwani">pehlwani</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilli-danda" title="Gilli-danda">gilli-danda</a></i>. Some of the earliest forms of Asian <a href="/wiki/Indian_martial_arts" title="Indian martial arts">martial arts</a>, such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalarippayattu" title="Kalarippayattu">kalarippayattu</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musti_yuddha" title="Musti yuddha">musti yuddha</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Silambam" title="Silambam">silambam</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marma_adi" title="Marma adi">marma adi</a></i>, originated in India. <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">Chess</a>, commonly held to have <a href="/wiki/History_of_chess#India" title="History of chess">originated in India</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/Chaturanga" title="Chaturanga">chaturaṅga</a></i>, is regaining widespread popularity with the rise in the number of Indian <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_(chess)" title="Grandmaster (chess)">grandmasters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolpert20032_349-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolpert20032-349">[340]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERediff_2008_b_350-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERediff_2008_b-350">[341]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Pachisi" title="Pachisi">Pachisi</a></i>, from which <a href="/wiki/Parcheesi" title="Parcheesi">parcheesi</a> derives, was played on a giant marble court by <a href="/wiki/Akbar" title="Akbar">Akbar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBinmore200798_351-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBinmore200798-351">[342]</a></sup></p>
<p>The improved results garnered by the <a href="/wiki/India_Davis_Cup_team" title="India Davis Cup team">Indian Davis Cup team</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Category:Indian_tennis_players" title="Category:Indian tennis players">Indian tennis players</a> in the early 2010s have made tennis increasingly popular in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Wall_Street_Journal_2009_352-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Wall_Street_Journal_2009-352">[343]</a></sup> India has a <a href="/wiki/Category:Indian_sport_shooters" title="Category:Indian sport shooters">comparatively strong presence</a> in shooting sports, and has won several medals at the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/ISSF_World_Shooting_Championships" title="ISSF World Shooting Championships">World Shooting Championships</a>, and the Commonwealth Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBritish_Broadcasting_Corporation_2010_b_353-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBritish_Broadcasting_Corporation_2010_b-353">[344]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Times_of_India_2010_354-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Times_of_India_2010-354">[345]</a></sup> Other sports in which Indians have succeeded internationally include <a href="/wiki/Badminton" title="Badminton">badminton</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBritish_Broadcasting_Corporation_2010_a_355-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBritish_Broadcasting_Corporation_2010_a-355">[346]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Saina_Nehwal" title="Saina Nehwal">Saina Nehwal</a> and <a href="/wiki/P._V._Sindhu" title="P. V. Sindhu">P V Sindhu</a> are two of the top ranked female badminton players in the world), boxing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMint_2010_356-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMint_2010-356">[347]</a></sup> and wrestling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEXavier2010_357-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXavier2010-357">[348]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Football_in_India" title="Football in India">Football</a> is popular in <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seven_Sister_States" title="Seven Sister States">north-eastern states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMajumdarBandyopadhyay20061–5_358-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMajumdarBandyopadhyay20061–5-358">[349]</a></sup> India is scheduled to host the <a href="/wiki/2017_FIFA_U-17_World_Cup" title="2017 FIFA U-17 World Cup">2017 FIFA U-17 World Cup</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-359"><a href="#cite_note-359">[350]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Field_hockey_in_India" title="Field hockey in India">Field hockey in India</a> is administered by <a href="/wiki/Hockey_India" title="Hockey India">Hockey India</a>. The <a href="/wiki/India_men%27s_national_field_hockey_team" title="India men's national field hockey team">Indian national hockey team</a> won the 1975 <a href="/wiki/Hockey_World_Cup" title="Hockey World Cup">Hockey World Cup</a> and have, as of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=India&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, taken eight gold, one silver, and two bronze Olympic medals, making it the sport's most successful team in the Olympics.</p>
<p>India has also played a major role in popularising <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a>. Thus, cricket is, by far, the most popular sport in India. The <a href="/wiki/India_national_cricket_team" title="India national cricket team">Indian national cricket team</a> won the <a href="/wiki/1983_Cricket_World_Cup" title="1983 Cricket World Cup">1983</a> and <a href="/wiki/2011_Cricket_World_Cup" title="2011 Cricket World Cup">2011 Cricket World Cup</a> events, the <a href="/wiki/2007_ICC_World_Twenty20" title="2007 ICC World Twenty20">2007 ICC World Twenty20</a>, shared the <a href="/wiki/2002_ICC_Champions_Trophy" title="2002 ICC Champions Trophy">2002 ICC Champions Trophy</a> with Sri Lanka, and won <a href="/wiki/2013_ICC_Champions_Trophy" title="2013 ICC Champions Trophy">2013 ICC Champions Trophy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cricket_in_India" title="Cricket in India">Cricket in India</a> is administered by the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Control_for_Cricket_in_India" title="Board of Control for Cricket in India">Board of Control for Cricket in India</a> (BCCI); the <a href="/wiki/Ranji_Trophy" title="Ranji Trophy">Ranji Trophy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Duleep_Trophy" title="Duleep Trophy">Duleep Trophy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Deodhar_Trophy" title="Deodhar Trophy">Deodhar Trophy</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irani_Trophy" title="Irani Trophy">Irani Trophy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/NKP_Salve_Challenger_Trophy" title="NKP Salve Challenger Trophy">NKP Salve Challenger Trophy</a> are domestic competitions. The BCCI also conducts an annual <a href="/wiki/Twenty20" title="Twenty20">Twenty20</a> competition known as the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Premier_League" title="Indian Premier League">Indian Premier League</a>.</p>
<p>India has hosted or co-hosted several international sporting events: the <a href="/wiki/1951_Asian_Games" title="1951 Asian Games">1951</a> and <a href="/wiki/1982_Asian_Games" title="1982 Asian Games">1982 Asian Games</a>; the <a href="/wiki/1987_Cricket_World_Cup" title="1987 Cricket World Cup">1987</a>, <a href="/wiki/1996_Cricket_World_Cup" title="1996 Cricket World Cup">1996</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2011_Cricket_World_Cup" title="2011 Cricket World Cup">2011 Cricket World Cup</a> tournaments; the <a href="/wiki/2003_Afro-Asian_Games" title="2003 Afro-Asian Games">2003 Afro-Asian Games</a>; the <a href="/wiki/2006_ICC_Champions_Trophy" title="2006 ICC Champions Trophy">2006 ICC Champions Trophy</a>; the <a href="/wiki/2010_Men%27s_Hockey_World_Cup" title="2010 Men's Hockey World Cup">2010 Hockey World Cup</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/2010_Commonwealth_Games" title="2010 Commonwealth Games">2010 Commonwealth Games</a>. Major international sporting events held annually in India include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chennai_Open" title="Chennai Open">Chennai Open</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mumbai_Marathon" title="Mumbai Marathon">Mumbai Marathon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Delhi_Half_Marathon" title="Delhi Half Marathon">Delhi Half Marathon</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Masters" title="Indian Masters">Indian Masters</a>. The first <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula 1</a> <a href="/wiki/Indian_Grand_Prix" title="Indian Grand Prix">Indian Grand Prix</a> featured in late 2011 but has been discontinued from the F1 season calendar since 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDehejia2011_360-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDehejia2011-360">[351]</a></sup></p>
<p>India has traditionally been the dominant country at the <a href="/wiki/South_Asian_Games" title="South Asian Games">South Asian Games</a>. An example of this dominance is the <a href="/wiki/Basketball_at_the_South_Asian_Games" title="Basketball at the South Asian Games">basketball competition</a> where <a href="/wiki/India_national_basketball_team" title="India national basketball team">Team India</a> won three out of four tournaments to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-361"><a href="#cite_note-361">[352]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rajiv_Gandhi_Khel_Ratna" title="Rajiv Gandhi Khel Ratna">Rajiv Gandhi Khel Ratna</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arjuna_Award" title="Arjuna Award">Arjuna Award</a> are the highest forms of government recognition for athletic achievement; the <a href="/wiki/Dronacharya_Award" title="Dronacharya Award">Dronacharya Award</a> is awarded for excellence in coaching.</p>
